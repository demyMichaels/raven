Welcome to Expense Accounts!

Expense accounts track all the money flowing OUT of a business. Understanding expenses is crucial for managing a business successfully.

Why Expense Accounts Matter:

  • They show where money is being spent
  • They help prepare financial statements
  • They're needed for calculating profit or loss
  • They're important for tax purposes

Salaries & Wages Account

What are Salaries & Wages?

The Salaries & Wages Account tracks money paid to employees for their work. This is usually one of the largest expenses for many businesses.

Salary

Fixed amount paid regularly (usually monthly) regardless of hours worked

Wages

Payment based on hours worked or pieces produced (usually weekly or daily)

Who's involved?

Employees Payroll Department Tax Authorities HR Department

Remember:

Salaries & wages are typically paid through bank transfers or checks, not usually in cash.

Common Questions

Salary is an expense because it represents money leaving the business to pay for employee services. This reduces the assets (cash) without directly creating a tangible asset in return.
Unlike asset accounts (like equipment) that represent things the business owns, salary expenses don't create ownership. They're payments for services already received, not investments in future value.
Salaries reduce profit directly. If a business earns $10,000 in revenue but pays $6,000 in salaries, that expense reduces the profit to $4,000 (before considering other expenses).

Rent Account

What is the Rent Account?

The Rent Account tracks money paid to use property owned by someone else, such as office space, buildings, or equipment.

Property Rent

Payment for using buildings, offices, or land

Equipment Rent

Payment for temporarily using machinery or equipment

Who's involved?

Landlords/Property Owners Rental Agencies Real Estate Companies Equipment Rental Companies

Important to know:

Rent is usually paid in advance (at the start of the month) and may include security deposits that are not part of the expense.

Transport and Travelling Account

What is the Transport and Travelling Account?

The Transport and Travelling Account records expenses related to business travel, including transportation costs, accommodations, and meals while traveling.

Transport Costs

Airfare, bus tickets, train tickets, fuel, vehicle rentals

Travel Expenses

Hotel stays, meals during travel, conference fees

Who's involved?

Airlines Hotels Car Rental Agencies Travel Agencies Restaurants Transportation Companies

Important to know:

Travel expenses should be supported with receipts and may require pre-approval in many organizations.

Transport vs. Other Expenses:

Not all movement costs are travel expenses!

These are NOT Travel Expenses:
  • Shipping goods to customers (Shipping Expense)
  • Delivery charges for supplies (Part of Purchase Cost)
  • Employee commuting (Personal Expense)

Printing and Stationery Account

What is the Printing and Stationery Account?

The Printing and Stationery Account tracks money spent on office supplies like paper, pens, notebooks, printing costs, and other everyday items needed to run the business.

Stationery

Paper, notebooks, pens, pencils, folders, clips, staplers, etc.

Printing

Printer ink, toner, printing services, photocopying costs, etc.

Who's involved?

Office Supply Stores Printing Companies Paper Manufacturers Stationery Wholesalers Copy Centers

Remember:

Small items like these are expenses, not assets, even though they may last for some time, because they're relatively inexpensive and are used up quickly.

Purchases Account

What is the Purchases Account?

The Purchases Account records money spent on buying goods that will be resold to customers. This is different from buying supplies (like stationery) that are used by the business itself.

Merchandise Purchases

Goods bought specifically to resell to customers

Raw Materials

Items bought to make into finished products for sale

Who's involved?

Suppliers Wholesalers Manufacturers Distributors Vendors

Important to understand:

Purchases are different from other expenses because the items bought are intended to be sold later for a profit. The cost becomes "Cost of Goods Sold" when the items are sold.

Important Note

Purchases vs. Expenses

There's an important difference between purchases and other expenses:

Purchases
  • Items bought to resell
  • Becomes "Cost of Goods Sold"
  • Directly related to sales
  • First becomes inventory
Other Expenses
  • Items used by the business
  • Stays as an expense
  • Indirectly supports business
  • Consumed immediately

Depreciation Account

What is the Depreciation Account?

The Depreciation Account records the reduction in value of assets over time due to wear and tear, obsolescence, or aging. It's like recording how your bicycle loses value as it gets older.

Straight-line Depreciation

Equal amount of depreciation each year over the asset's useful life

Accumulated Depreciation

Total depreciation recorded for an asset since purchase

Who's involved?

Asset Suppliers Maintenance Vendors Repair Services Asset Disposal Companies

Important to understand:

Depreciation is a non-cash expense. You don't actually pay out money when recording depreciation, but it still reduces your profit and the book value of assets.

Educational Note

Why Depreciation Matters:

  • It matches the cost of using assets with the periods when they generate revenue
  • It helps show the true financial position of the business
  • It helps plan for asset replacements
  • It has tax implications in many countries

Key Formula:

Annual Straight-Line Depreciation =

(Purchase Cost - Salvage Value) Useful Life

Example:

If a school computer lab costs $25,000, has an estimated salvage value of $5,000 after 5 years, the annual depreciation would be:

($25,000 - $5,000) 5 = $4,000 per year

Closing the Books

What is Closing the Books?

Closing the books is the process of transferring all temporary account balances (income, expenses, etc.) to the permanent equity account (Retained Earnings or Capital) at the end of an accounting period. This resets temporary accounts to zero for the next period.

Temporary Accounts

Revenue, expense, and drawing accounts that are closed at the end of each period

Permanent Accounts

Assets, liabilities, and equity accounts that carry balances to the next period

Who's involved?

Accountants Bookkeepers Business Owners Financial Managers

Why closing matters:

Closing the books allows for accurate financial reporting for each period, provides a clean slate for the next period, and helps track profitability over time.

Before vs. After Closing

Before Closing:
  • Expense accounts show year's activity
  • Revenue accounts show year's earnings
  • Balances reflect entire period
After Closing:
  • All expense accounts reduced to zero
  • All revenue accounts reduced to zero
  • Net income/loss transferred to Retained Earnings
  • Ready for new accounting period

Important Concepts

Income Summary Account:

A temporary account used only during the closing process to gather all revenue and expense account balances before transferring the net result to Retained Earnings.

Permanent vs. Temporary Accounts:

Permanent Accounts:
  • Assets
  • Liabilities
  • Equity/Retained Earnings
Temporary Accounts:
  • Revenue
  • Expenses
  • Gains and Losses
  • Dividends/Withdrawals
