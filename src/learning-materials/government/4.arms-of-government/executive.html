üèõ What is the Executive?

The executive arm of government is the organ responsible for policy-making and implementation of policies and laws made in the country. The executive arm is the same as administration or cabinet.

üéØ Key Points to Remember:

  • The executive implements laws and policies
  • It carries out day-to-day government work
  • Also known as administration or cabinet
  • Its functions depend on the type of constitution

Who Belongs to the Executive?

üë§ Political Leaders

Presidents, Prime Ministers, Governors, Ministers

üìã Civil Service

Top civil servants and administrative officials

üõ° Security Forces

Armed forces, police, and law enforcement agencies

üí° Did You Know? In some countries, the executive can perform legislative and judicial functions. For example, presidents can pardon offenders and appoint supreme court judges!

‚öôÔ∏è Functions of the Executive

The executive arm performs numerous critical functions in government. Here are the main ones:

  • 1. Policy Formulation üìù

    Creating and developing policies that guide government action and address national issues.

  • 2. Supervision üëÅ

    Supervising administrative functions and directing the execution of laws made in the state.

  • 3. Appointment of Top Officials üëî

    The power to appoint and remove top officials of the state from government positions.

  • 4. Declaration of War ‚öîÔ∏è

    Can declare war, maintain peace, and fight back foreign invasion to protect the nation.

  • 5. Representation üåç

    Represents the state in its relations with other countries and international organizations.

  • 6. Power of Pardon üïä

    Pardons those who have offended the state, showing mercy where appropriate.

  • 7. Initiation of Bills üìú

    Initiates bills, defends them, and makes recommendations on proposed legislation.

  • 8. Budgeting üí∞

    Prepares and delivers the annual income and expenditure budget of the state.

  • 9. Maintenance of Law and Order üëÆ

    Law enforcement agents (police) are responsible for maintaining peace and order.

  • 10. Signing Bills into Law ‚úçÔ∏è

    For a bill to become a law, the president must sign it into effect.

  • 11. Enacting Laws üìñ

    Enacts laws to promote good governance and effective administration.

  • 12. Protection of Rights ‚öñÔ∏è

    Government guarantees the fundamental human rights of its citizens.

‚ö†Ô∏è Study Tip: Try to memorize at least 6 main functions. Use the acronym "PSAD-RIBE": Policy, Supervision, Appointments, Declaration of war, Representation, Initiation of bills, Budgeting, Enforcement of law.

üîç Types of Executives

Executives can be classified in different ways. Let's explore the main classifications:

Classification 1: Based on Power Distribution

üë§ Single Executive

Definition: Control of the executive rests with one individual.

Example: The President of the USA has ministers named by him who are his advisers and agents, not his colleagues.

Key Feature: One person holds ultimate authority and makes final decisions.

üë• Plural Executive

Definition: Executive power is shared among multiple people.

Example: The President of the Swiss Confederation (Switzerland) is the chairman of the federal council and exercises the usual powers of a chairman. Other members are his colleagues, not agents or advisers.

Key Feature: Power is distributed among equals who work together.

Classification 2: Based on Relationship with Legislature

üèõ Parliamentary Executive

Definition: The executive is chosen from parliament and holds office only as long as it commands the confidence of that parliament.

Example: The executive in Britain (UK)

Key Features:

  • Executive members are also legislators
  • Can be removed by vote of no confidence
  • Close relationship between executive and legislature

üó≥ Non-Parliamentary Executive

Definition: The executive is chosen independently of the legislature and holds office for a fixed term.

Example: The executive in the USA

Key Features:

  • Separate from the legislature
  • Fixed term of office
  • Clear separation of powers

üí™ Increased Powers of the Executive

Over time, the executive has gained more powers. Here's why and how:

  • 1. Power to Initiate Bills üìù

    The executive has significant influence in law-making through its power to introduce new bills to the legislature.

  • 2. Power of Delegated Legislation ‚öñÔ∏è

    The complexity of parliament's work allows the executive to make rules, regulations, and statutory instruments on specific matters without going through full legislative process.

  • 3. Control of Armed Forces and Police üõ°

    The executive controls security forces and other instruments, adding to its influence and power in modern government.

  • 4. Quasi-Judicial Powers ‚öñÔ∏è

    For instance, the power of pardon (prerogative of mercy) has added to the growth of executive powers.

  • 5. The Issue of Dependence üîó

    The judiciary and legislature depend on the executive to implement their decisions, giving the executive significant leverage.

  • 6. Emergency Periods ‚ö†Ô∏è

    In modern government, speed is required in decision-making and implementation, especially during emergencies when the executive exercises both legislative and executive functions.

  • 7. Policy-Making üìã

    Policies implemented by the executive, decided at party levels, sometimes erode the powers of the legislature.

ü§î Think About It: While increased executive power can lead to faster decision-making, it also raises concerns about the balance of power. Can too much executive power be dangerous? What safeguards are needed?

üîí Executive Controls

To prevent abuse of power, various mechanisms control the executive. Here are the main controls:

  • 1. The Constitution üìú

    Imposes limitations on executive power. The executive cannot act "ultra vires" (beyond its powers).

  • 2. Judicial Challenges ‚öñÔ∏è

    Executive actions can be challenged in courts and declared illegal, null and void, or unconstitutional.

  • 3. Judicial Applications üìã

    Writs such as habeas corpus, mandamus, and injunctions can compel the executive to act or refrain from acting.

  • 4. Legislative Approval üó≥

    In presidential systems, some executive appointments and decisions must be approved by the legislature.

  • 5. Impeachment / Vote of No Confidence üö´

    In presidential systems, the president can be impeached. In parliamentary systems, a vote of no confidence can remove the executive.

  • 6. Change of Government üîÑ

    If government becomes unpopular or unreasonable, change can occur through peaceful or violent means.

  • 7. Shadow Cabinet / Opposition üë•

    In parliamentary systems, the opposition subjects the executive to constant watch, scrutiny, and criticism.

  • 8. Party Caucuses üèõ

    Through party caucuses and representation in legislature (e.g., House of Commons in Britain), the executive is controlled.

  • 9. Free and Fair Elections üó≥

    Periodic elections allow the electorate to control the executive through referendum or plebiscite.

  • 10. The Media üì∞

    As the "watchdog" and "fourth estate of the realm," mass media controls the executive by highlighting its activities.

  • 11. International Treaties üåç

    International agreements (UN, Commonwealth, AU, Amnesty International, ILO, IMF) can influence executive actions.

  • 12. Conventions and Norms üìñ

    Events like war, crises, state of emergency, and disasters can influence or control the executive.

  • 13. International Sanctions üö®

    Sanctions, bans, embargoes, and boycotts can force the executive to take specific positions on issues.

üéØ Why Are Controls Important?

  • Prevent Dictatorship: No single branch should have absolute power
  • Protect Rights: Ensure citizens' rights are not violated
  • Ensure Accountability: The executive must answer for its actions
  • Maintain Balance: Keep the separation of powers working effectively
