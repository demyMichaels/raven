Classification of Waves

Waves can be classified based on their nature, the direction of particle vibration, and their energy transfer characteristics. This page covers the fundamental classifications required for secondary school physics (JAMB/WAEC/NECO).

📌 Learning Objectives

Candidates should be able to:

  • distinguish between mechanical and electromagnetic waves
  • differentiate between longitudinal and transverse waves
  • distinguish between stationary and progressive waves
  • give examples of waves from springs, ropes, stretched strings and the ripple tank
  • identify the type of wave produced in different media
  • explain the formation of stationary waves

i. Mechanical vs Electromagnetic Waves

Waves are broadly divided based on whether they require a medium to travel.

Mechanical WavesElectromagnetic Waves
Require a material medium (solid, liquid, or gas).Can travel through vacuum; no medium needed.
Examples: sound waves, water waves, seismic waves, waves on a string.Examples: light, radio waves, X‑rays, microwaves, ultraviolet.
Speed depends on the elastic and inertial properties of the medium.Speed in vacuum is constant (c ≈ 3.0 × 10⁸ m/s).
Cannot travel through vacuum.Can travel through vacuum and through some media (slower than in vacuum).

ii. Longitudinal and Transverse Waves

This classification is based on the direction of vibration of particles relative to the direction of wave propagation.

Transverse Waves

In transverse waves, particles vibrate perpendicular to the direction of wave travel.

  • Crest: the maximum displacement point above the equilibrium position.
  • Trough: the maximum displacement point below the equilibrium position.
  • Examples: waves on a string, light (electromagnetic), water waves (surface waves).

Longitudinal Waves

In longitudinal waves, particles vibrate parallel to the direction of wave travel.

  • Compression: region where particles are closest together (high pressure).
  • Rarefaction: region where particles are farthest apart (low pressure).
  • Examples: sound waves in air, ultrasound, compression waves in a spring.

iii. Stationary vs Progressive Waves

Progressive waves transfer energy from one point to another, while stationary waves are formed by the superposition of two waves of same frequency and amplitude traveling in opposite directions.

Progressive WaveStationary Wave
Energy is transmitted from one place to another.Energy is confined within the medium; no net energy transfer.
All particles oscillate with the same amplitude (except for phase differences).Different particles have different amplitudes: nodes (zero amplitude) and antinodes (maximum amplitude) are formed.
Phase changes continuously along the wave.All particles between two nodes are in phase; particles on opposite sides of a node are out of phase.
Equation: y = A sin(ωt – kx) or y = A sin(ωt + kx).Equation: y = 2A sin(kx) cos(ωt) (result of superposition).
Example: wave on a long rope.Example: vibration of a stretched string fixed at both ends (musical instruments).

iv. Examples from Springs, Ropes, Stretched Strings, and Ripple Tank

Springs (Slinky)

  • Transverse wave: Shake one end of the spring sideways – a transverse pulse travels along the spring.
  • Longitudinal wave: Push and pull one end – compressions and rarefactions travel along the spring.

Ropes / Stretched Strings

  • A flick or pluck at one end sends a transverse wave along the rope.
  • If the rope is fixed at both ends, standing waves (harmonics) can be set up at specific frequencies (fundamental, overtones).

Ripple Tank

  • A ripple tank is used to demonstrate water wave phenomena.
  • A vibrating dipper creates circular or plane waves.
  • It shows reflection (from barriers), refraction (by changing depth), diffraction (through gaps), and interference (from two dippers).
  • Water waves are mainly transverse (with some longitudinal components near the surface).

Quick Reference:

  • Spring: both transverse and longitudinal waves can be produced.
  • Rope/String: transverse waves (progressive and standing).
  • Ripple tank: surface water waves (mainly transverse).

Check Your Understanding

Q1: What type of wave is produced when you move one end of a stretched spring up and down rapidly?

Answer: Transverse wave.

Q2: Can sound waves travel through vacuum? Why?

Answer: No, sound is a mechanical wave and requires a material medium.

Q3: In a stationary wave on a string, what are nodes and antinodes?

Answer: Nodes are points of zero displacement; antinodes are points of maximum displacement.
