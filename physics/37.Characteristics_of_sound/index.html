Characteristics of Sound Waves

Sound waves have distinctive properties that determine how we perceive them. This page covers the differences between noise and musical notes, the physical attributes of sound, harmonics in strings and air columns, resonance, and the calculation of frequencies in pipes.

📌 Learning Objectives

Candidates should be able to:

  • differentiate between noise and musical notes
  • analyse quality, pitch, intensity and loudness of sound
  • apply these concepts to musical instruments
  • identify overtones produced by vibrating strings and air columns
  • itemize acoustical examples of resonance
  • determine frequencies of notes emitted by open and closed pipes in relation to their lengths

i. Noise and Musical Notes

Musical note: A sound produced by regular, periodic vibrations. It has a definite pitch and waveform that repeats regularly (e.g., sound from a tuning fork, flute, or violin).

Noise: A sound produced by irregular, non‑periodic vibrations. It consists of many frequencies mixed randomly and lacks a definite pitch (e.g., the sound of a door slamming, traffic noise, static).

Musical NoteNoise
Periodic waveformNon‑periodic, irregular waveform
Pleasant to hearUsually unpleasant
Definite pitch and frequencyNo definite pitch
Examples: sitar, piano, whistleExamples: thunder, scratching, crowd murmur

ii. Quality, Pitch, Intensity and Loudness

These are the key characteristics that distinguish one sound from another.

  • Pitch: Determined by frequency. Higher frequency → higher pitch. (e.g., female voice has higher pitch than male voice).
  • Intensity: The rate of energy flow per unit area (power/area). Measured in W/m². It is a physical quantity.
  • Loudness: The physiological sensation of intensity. It depends on intensity but also on the ear's sensitivity. It is measured in decibels (dB).
  • Quality (timbre): The characteristic that distinguishes two sounds of the same pitch and loudness. It depends on the number and relative intensities of overtones (harmonics) present. For example, a flute and a violin playing the same note sound different due to different harmonic content.
Application to musical instruments: The quality (timbre) of an instrument is shaped by its construction (material, shape) which emphasizes certain overtones. Pitch is varied by changing the length of the vibrating element (strings, air columns) or tension. Loudness is controlled by the amplitude of vibration (how hard you blow or strike).

iii. Harmonics and Overtones in Strings and Air Columns

When a string or air column vibrates, it can vibrate in multiple modes simultaneously. The lowest frequency is the fundamental (first harmonic). Higher frequencies are called overtones. Overtones that are integer multiples of the fundamental are called harmonics.

Vibrating Strings (fixed at both ends)

For a string of length L, tension T, and linear density μ, the fundamental frequency is:

f₁ = (1/2L) √(T/μ)

Harmonics: fₙ = n f₁ , where n = 1,2,3,…
The string vibrates with nodes at both ends. The first harmonic (fundamental) has one antinode; second harmonic has two antinodes, etc.

[Diagram: Standing waves on a string for n=1,2,3]

Air Columns

Air columns in pipes can be open at both ends or closed at one end.

  • Open pipe (both ends open): Antinodes at both ends. Harmonics: fₙ = n f₁, n=1,2,3,… (all harmonics present).
  • Closed pipe (one end closed): Node at closed end, antinode at open end. Harmonics: fₙ = n f₁, but only odd n (1,3,5,…) are present.

Overtones: In open pipes, first overtone = second harmonic, second overtone = third harmonic, etc. In closed pipes, first overtone = third harmonic, second overtone = fifth harmonic, etc.

iv. Acoustic Examples of Resonance

Resonance occurs when a system is driven at its natural frequency, leading to large amplitude vibrations. Acoustic examples:

  • Tuning fork and resonance box: When a tuning fork is placed on a resonance box, the air column inside vibrates sympathetically, amplifying the sound.
  • Shattering a glass with sound: If a singer hits the natural frequency of a glass, resonance can cause it to vibrate violently and break.
  • Musical instruments: The air column in a flute or organ pipe resonates at specific frequencies to produce notes. The body of a guitar or violin resonates to amplify and enrich the sound.
  • Two identical tuning forks: If one is struck and then silenced, the other (if placed nearby) will start vibrating due to resonance.
  • Microwave oven: Water molecules resonate at microwave frequency, causing heating.

v. Frequency of Air Columns (Open and Closed Pipes)

The frequency depends on the length of the pipe and whether it is open or closed.

Pipe TypeFundamental frequency (f₁)Harmonics
Open pipe (both ends open) f₁ = v / (2L) (v = speed of sound) fₙ = n f₁, n = 1,2,3,… (all harmonics)
Closed pipe (one end closed) f₁ = v / (4L) fₙ = n f₁, but only odd n (1,3,5,…)

Where L is the length of the pipe, v is the speed of sound in air (≈ 340 m/s at room temperature).

Example 1: An open organ pipe is 0.5 m long. Calculate the fundamental frequency (take v = 340 m/s).

Solution: f₁ = v/(2L) = 340 / (2×0.5) = 340 Hz.

Example 2: A closed pipe of length 0.25 m produces its first resonance. Find the frequency. (v = 340 m/s).

Solution: For closed pipe, fundamental f₁ = v/(4L) = 340/(4×0.25) = 340 Hz.

Example 3: What is the second overtone of a closed pipe of length 0.3 m? (v = 340 m/s).

Solution: For closed pipe, harmonics are odd: 1st (fundamental), 3rd (1st overtone), 5th (2nd overtone). So 2nd overtone = 5th harmonic. f₅ = 5f₁ = 5 × (340/(4×0.3)) = 5 × 283.33 ≈ 1417 Hz.

Check Your Understanding

Q1: Distinguish between noise and a musical note.

Answer: Noise is irregular, non‑periodic vibration; musical note is regular and periodic.

Q2: What determines the pitch of a sound?

Answer: Frequency – higher frequency gives higher pitch.

Q3: Why does a closed pipe only produce odd harmonics?

Answer: Because the closed end is a displacement node and the open end is an antinode; the length must be an odd multiple of λ/4.

Q4: Give two examples of acoustic resonance.

Answer: Tuning fork and resonance box, sound production in musical instruments.

Q5: An open pipe has length 0.68 m. Find its fundamental frequency (v = 340 m/s).

Answer: f₁ = 340/(2×0.68) = 340/1.36 = 250 Hz.
