Propagation of Sound

Sound is a mechanical wave that requires a material medium for its propagation. This page covers the fundamental principles of sound propagation, speed in different media, reflection phenomena (echoes and reverberation), and their practical applications.

📌 Learning Objectives

Candidates should be able to:

  • determine the need for a material medium in the propagation of sound waves
  • compare the speed of sound in solids, liquids and air
  • relate the effects of temperature and pressure to the speed of sound in air
  • solve problems on echoes, reverberation and speed
  • compare the disadvantages and advantages of echoes
  • explain applications of echoes and reverberation

i. Necessity for a Material Medium

Sound is a mechanical wave, meaning it requires a material medium (solid, liquid, or gas) to travel. It cannot propagate through a vacuum because there are no particles to transmit the vibrations.

Experiment: An electric bell is placed inside a glass jar and the air is gradually removed. As the air is pumped out, the sound becomes fainter until it is no longer heard, demonstrating that sound needs a medium.

Key point: Sound travels through solids, liquids, and gases, but not through vacuum.

ii. Speed of Sound in Solids, Liquids and Air

The speed of sound depends on the elasticity and density of the medium. In general, sound travels fastest in solids, slower in liquids, and slowest in gases.

MediumTypical Speed (m/s)Reason
Solids (e.g., steel)~5000 – 6000High elasticity; particles are closely packed.
Liquids (e.g., water)~1500Moderate elasticity; particles less tightly bound than solids.
Gases (e.g., air at 0°C)~331Low elasticity; particles far apart.

Formula for speed in air: v = 331 + 0.6T (where T is temperature in °C). Speed increases with temperature.

Effect of pressure: At constant temperature, speed of sound in air is independent of pressure (because density changes proportionally with pressure).

iii. Reflection of Sound: Echoes, Reverberation and Applications

Sound waves reflect off hard surfaces (e.g., walls, cliffs) just like light reflects off mirrors.

Echo

An echo is a distinct, reflected sound heard after the original sound. It occurs when the reflecting surface is far enough (typically >17 m) that the reflected sound arrives after the original sound has ceased (persistence of hearing ~0.1 s).

Distance calculation: If the speed of sound is v and the time between the original sound and echo is t, then the distance to the reflecting surface d is: d = (v × t) / 2 (because sound travels to the surface and back).

Reverberation

Reverberation is the persistence of sound in an enclosed space due to multiple reflections. It is the "echo" effect you hear in a large hall or auditorium where sound lingers for a short time. Reverberation time is the time taken for the sound to decay by 60 dB.

Applications of echoes and reverberation:

  • Sonar (Sound Navigation and Ranging): Ships use echoes of ultrasonic waves to detect underwater objects and measure depth.
  • Echo sounding / Depth finding: Determining ocean depth using reflected sound.
  • Medical imaging (ultrasound): Echoes of high-frequency sound waves create images of internal organs.
  • Architectural acoustics: Controlling reverberation in concert halls, auditoriums, and recording studios to enhance sound quality.
  • Bats and dolphins: Use echolocation (echoes) to navigate and hunt.

iv. Advantages and Disadvantages of Echoes and Reverberations

PhenomenonAdvantagesDisadvantages
Echo
  • Used in sonar, ultrasound, depth sounding.
  • Used in navigation by bats/dolphins.
  • Can be used to measure distance (e.g., cliff or wall).
  • In telephone/radio communication, echoes cause distortion.
  • Unwanted echoes in large empty halls can make speech unclear.
Reverberation
  • Adds richness to music (concerts, churches).
  • Improves sound quality in auditoriums when controlled.
  • Used in sound recording to create ambiance.
  • Excessive reverberation causes speech to become indistinct (e.g., in large empty halls).
  • Interferes with clarity in classrooms or conference rooms.
  • In recording studios, unwanted reverberation can ruin a take.

Numerical Problems on Echoes and Speed

Example 1: A man stands between two cliffs and claps his hands. He hears the first echo after 2 seconds and the second echo after 3 seconds. If the speed of sound is 340 m/s, find the distance between the cliffs.

Solution: Distance to first cliff d₁ = (v × t₁)/2 = (340 × 2)/2 = 340 m. Distance to second cliff d₂ = (340 × 3)/2 = 510 m. Distance between cliffs = d₁ + d₂ = 340 + 510 = 850 m.

Example 2: A ship sends out an ultrasound pulse and receives its echo from the seabed after 4 seconds. If the speed of sound in water is 1500 m/s, find the depth of the sea.

Solution: Depth d = (v × t)/2 = (1500 × 4)/2 = 3000 m.

Example 3: What is the minimum distance to hear a distinct echo if the speed of sound is 340 m/s and the persistence of hearing is 0.1 s?

Solution: For echo to be distinct, the reflected sound must arrive after 0.1 s. Distance = (v × t)/2 = (340 × 0.1)/2 = 17 m.

Effect of Temperature and Pressure on Speed of Sound in Air

  • Temperature: Speed increases with temperature. v ∝ √T (in Kelvin). Approximate formula: v = 331 + 0.6T (T in °C).
  • Pressure: At constant temperature, speed is independent of pressure. Reason: Increase in pressure increases density proportionally, so v = √(γP/ρ) remains constant.

Check Your Understanding

Q1: Why can sound not travel through a vacuum?

Answer: Sound is a mechanical wave and requires particles to transmit vibrations; vacuum has no particles.

Q2: In which medium does sound travel fastest: air, water, or steel?

Answer: Steel (solids).

Q3: How does an increase in temperature affect the speed of sound in air?

Answer: Speed increases by approximately 0.6 m/s per °C.

Q4: A person shouts in a valley and hears an echo after 2.5 s. If the speed of sound is 340 m/s, how far away is the cliff?

Answer: d = (340 × 2.5)/2 = 425 m.

Q5: Give one advantage and one disadvantage of reverberation.

Answer: Advantage: enhances music in concert halls. Disadvantage: can make speech unclear if excessive.
