Production and Propagation of Waves

Wave motion is a fundamental concept in physics, describing how energy travels through a medium or vacuum. This page covers everything from basic definitions to advanced wave phenomena, aligned with JAMB/WAEC/NECO syllabi.

1. Wave Motion

A wave is a disturbance that travels through a medium (or vacuum) transferring energy from one point to another without the permanent displacement of the particles of the medium. In wave motion, it is the energy that moves, not the matter.

Key idea: In a wave, particles oscillate about their mean positions but do not travel with the wave.

2. Vibrating Systems as Sources of Waves

Any object that vibrates can generate waves. Examples:

  • Tuning fork: prongs vibrate, producing sound waves in air.
  • Loudspeaker: diaphragm vibrates, creating compressions and rarefactions.
  • String instruments: plucked strings vibrate, transferring energy to the air.
  • Earthquake: vibrations in the Earth's crust generate seismic waves.
  • Ripple tank: a vibrating dipper creates water waves.

3. Waves as a Mode of Energy Transfer

Waves carry energy without transporting matter. The energy of a wave depends on its amplitude and frequency. For example:

  • Sound waves transfer energy from a source to a listener.
  • Light waves from the Sun carry energy across space.
  • Ocean waves can erode coastlines.

Particles move from one place to another.Particles oscillate about fixed positions.
Involves transfer of matter.Involves transfer of energy only.
Example: a ball thrown through air.Example: a wave on a string.

5. Relationship between Frequency, Wavelength and Wave Velocity

The fundamental wave equation is:

v = f λ

where v = wave velocity (m/s), f = frequency (Hz), λ = wavelength (m).

For all waves, velocity depends on the medium (and for light, on the refractive index).

6. Phase Difference, Wave Number, Wave Vector

  • Phase difference (Δφ): The amount by which one wave leads or lags behind another. Measured in radians or degrees. Two points on a wave separated by a distance Δx have a phase difference Δφ = (2π/λ) Δx.
  • Wave number (k): Spatial frequency of a wave, defined as k = 2π/λ (radians per meter).
  • Wave vector (k): A vector whose magnitude is the wave number and direction is the direction of wave propagation.
k = 2π/λ    Δφ = k Δx (for same direction)

7. Progressive Wave Equation

A progressive wave (traveling wave) transfers energy from one point to another. Its general form is:

y(x,t) = A sin(ωt ± kx + φ₀)

Where:

  • y = displacement of particle at position x and time t
  • A = amplitude
  • ω = angular frequency = 2πf
  • k = wave number = 2π/λ
  • φ₀ = initial phase
  • Sign: – for wave traveling in +x direction, + for –x direction.

8. Examples in Wave Equations

Example 1: A wave is described by y = 0.05 sin(10πt – 2πx). Find amplitude, frequency, wavelength, and velocity.

Solution: Compare with y = A sin(ωt – kx).
A = 0.05 m, ω = 10π → f = ω/2π = 5 Hz, k = 2π → λ = 2π/k = 1 m, v = fλ = 5 m/s.

10. Longitudinal vs Transverse Waves

  • Transverse waves: Particles vibrate perpendicular to direction of wave propagation. Examples: waves on a string, electromagnetic waves.
  • Longitudinal waves: Particles vibrate parallel to direction of wave propagation. Examples: sound waves in air, compression waves in a spring.

12. Examples from Springs, Ropes, Strings, Ripple Tank

  • Spring: Push and pull generates longitudinal waves; shake end generates transverse waves.
  • Rope/String: Plucking or flicking creates transverse waves.
  • Ripple tank: Vibrating bar creates circular or plane water waves; demonstrates reflection, refraction, diffraction.

13. Wave Phenomena: Reflection, Refraction, Diffraction, Polarization

  • Reflection: Waves bounce off a barrier. Angle of incidence = angle of reflection.
  • Refraction: Change in wave speed and direction when entering a different medium. (v = fλ, frequency constant).
  • Diffraction: Spreading of waves around obstacles or through apertures. Noticeable when gap size ≈ wavelength.
  • Polarization: Restriction of vibration to a single plane. Only transverse waves (e.g., light) can be polarized; longitudinal waves (sound) cannot.

14. Principle of Superposition of Waves

When two or more waves meet at a point, the resultant displacement is the vector sum of the individual displacements. This leads to interference:

  • Constructive interference: Waves in phase (path difference = nλ) → amplitude increases.
  • Destructive interference: Waves out of phase (path difference = (n+½)λ) → amplitude decreases or cancels.

15. Beats and Beat Frequency

Beats occur when two sound waves of slightly different frequencies interfere. The loudness waxes and wanes periodically. Beat frequency:

fbeat = |f₁ – f₂|

Uses: Tuning musical instruments, detecting small frequency differences.

16. Doppler Effect of Sound and Applications

The apparent change in frequency of a wave due to relative motion between source and observer.

  • Source moving towards observer → frequency increases (higher pitch).
  • Source moving away → frequency decreases.

Formula (sound): f' = f (v ± vo) / (v ∓ vs) where v is speed of sound, vo observer speed, vs source speed.

Applications: Radar speed guns, medical ultrasound (Doppler flow meters), astronomy (redshift).

Numerical Problems on Waves

Problem 1: A wave has a frequency of 50 Hz and a wavelength of 0.1 m. Calculate its velocity.

Solution: v = fλ = 50 × 0.1 = 5 m/s.

Problem 2: Two waves of frequencies 256 Hz and 260 Hz are sounded together. Find the beat frequency.

Solution: f_beat = |260 – 256| = 4 Hz.

Problem 3: A wave is represented by y = 0.02 sin(300t – 15x). Find amplitude, frequency, wavelength, and velocity.

Solution: A = 0.02 m; ω = 300 → f = 300/(2π) ≈ 47.7 Hz; k = 15 → λ = 2π/15 ≈ 0.419 m; v = fλ ≈ 20 m/s (or v = ω/k = 300/15 = 20 m/s).
