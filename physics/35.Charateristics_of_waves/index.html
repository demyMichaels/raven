Characteristics / Properties of Waves

Waves exhibit various phenomena when they interact with obstacles, boundaries, or other waves. Understanding these properties is essential for explaining natural phenomena and technological applications.

📌 Learning Objectives

Candidates should be able to:

  • describe reflection, refraction, diffraction and plane polarization of waves
  • explain the principle of superposition of waves
  • distinguish between constructive and destructive interference
  • explain the formation of beats and determine beat frequency
  • describe the Doppler effect qualitatively and give examples
  • solve problems involving these wave properties

i. Reflection, Refraction, Diffraction and Plane Polarization

Reflection

Reflection occurs when a wave hits a boundary or obstacle and bounces back. The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection (for smooth surfaces). Examples: echo (sound), mirror image (light), water waves reflecting off a barrier.

Refraction

Refraction is the change in direction of a wave when it passes from one medium to another due to a change in speed. Frequency remains constant, but wavelength and velocity change. Examples: bending of light in water, sound waves bending in different temperature layers.

Diffraction

Diffraction is the spreading out of waves when they pass through an aperture or around an obstacle. The effect is most noticeable when the aperture size is comparable to the wavelength. Examples: sound waves bending around a door, light spreading through a narrow slit.

Plane Polarization

Polarization is the restriction of vibrations of a transverse wave to a single plane. Only transverse waves (like light) can be polarized; longitudinal waves (sound) cannot. Polarization occurs when waves pass through a polarizing filter or reflect off surfaces. Examples: Polaroid sunglasses, 3D movie glasses, antenna orientation.

ii. Superposition of Waves e.g. Interference

The principle of superposition states that when two or more waves meet at a point, the resultant displacement is the vector sum of the individual displacements. This leads to interference.

  • Constructive interference: occurs when waves are in phase (path difference = nλ) – amplitudes add, resulting in a larger amplitude.
  • Destructive interference: occurs when waves are out of phase (path difference = (n+½)λ) – amplitudes cancel, resulting in smaller or zero amplitude.

Examples: Young's double-slit experiment (light), interference patterns from two speakers (sound), thin film colors (soap bubbles).

Important condition for observable interference: The sources must be coherent (same frequency and constant phase difference).

iii. Beats

Beats are periodic variations in loudness (or intensity) heard when two sound waves of slightly different frequencies interfere. The frequency of the beats (beat frequency) equals the difference between the two frequencies:

Beat frequency: fbeat = |f₁ – f₂|

Explanation: When two waves with frequencies f₁ and f₂ superpose, they alternately reinforce and cancel, producing a waxing and waning sound. Beats are used in tuning musical instruments and detecting small frequency differences.

Example: If two tuning forks of frequencies 256 Hz and 260 Hz are sounded together, the beat frequency is 4 Hz (four beats per second).

iv. Doppler Effect (qualitative treatment only)

The Doppler effect is the apparent change in frequency of a wave due to relative motion between the source and the observer. It occurs for all types of waves (sound, light, etc.).

  • Source moving towards observer: Waves are compressed, wavelength decreases → frequency increases (higher pitch for sound; blueshift for light).
  • Source moving away from observer: Waves are stretched, wavelength increases → frequency decreases (lower pitch; redshift).
  • Observer moving relative to source: Similar effect; the relative speed matters.

Everyday examples: Siren of an ambulance sounds higher as it approaches and lower as it recedes. Radar speed guns use Doppler shift of radio waves. In astronomy, redshift of galaxies indicates they are moving away (expanding universe).

Qualitative summary: The frequency increases when source and observer approach each other; decreases when they move apart.

Check Your Understanding

Q1: What is the difference between constructive and destructive interference?

Answer: Constructive occurs when waves are in phase, amplitudes add; destructive occurs when waves are out of phase, amplitudes cancel.

Q2: Why can sound waves not be polarized?

Answer: Because sound waves are longitudinal; polarization requires transverse vibrations.

Q3: A stationary observer hears a frequency drop as a source passes by. What wave property does this illustrate?

Answer: Doppler effect.

Q4: Two tuning forks of frequencies 512 Hz and 508 Hz are sounded together. Calculate the beat frequency.

Answer: f_beat = |512 – 508| = 4 Hz.
