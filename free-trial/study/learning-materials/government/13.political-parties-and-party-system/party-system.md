        <section class="content-section definition">
            <h2>9.2 PARTY SYSTEM</h2>
            <p><strong>Definition:</strong> Party system may be defined as the conduct of government based on the number of political parties within the system, for example, NPN, UPN, NPP, GNPP, PRP and NAP, the six political parties of the Second Republic (1979-83). The six political parties are an example of multi-party system. There are three types of party systems: one-party, two-party and multi-party systems.</p>
        </section>
        
        <section class="content-section one-party">
            <h2>9.3 ONE-PARTY OR SINGLE PARTY SYSTEM</h2>
            <p><strong>Definition:</strong> Single party may be defined as one in which only one legal and legitimate political party is constitutionally recognised in the country. No other party is recognised and can therefore not exist. A one-party system is a common feature of communist or socialist countries, for example, North Korea, Cuba and China. In Africa, Malawi, Kenya, Zambia, etc. were formerly one-party states.</p>
            
            <h3>Types of One-party System</h3>
            <ol>
                <li><strong>True single-party system:</strong> The law effectively prevents the formation of any other active parties. By definition, this party system does not accept or permit opposition. This type of system was operative in Malawi, Zambia, etc.</li>
                <li><strong>Hegemonic party:</strong> One party is firmly established in office. The party has hegemony, that is, it enjoys unchallenged authority. The law does not operate formally against any opposition, but clearly the state concerned is effectively single party for period of many years. For example, South Africa was an example of a hegemonic party (all - white electorate). The National Party came into power in 1948, and it only left power when Dr Nelson Mandela, was elected president in 1994.</li>
                <li><strong>Predominant party:</strong> This is a party which is elected and continues to be elected because the electorate so desire. This type of party is mostly found in Asian countries.</li>
            </ol>
            
            <h3>Characteristics of a One-party State</h3>
            <ol>
                <li>One officially recognised party: There is no other officially recognised political party, apart from the legally recognised party that can contest elections.</li>
                <li>Recognised by the constitution: Only this legal party is recognised by the constitution.</li>
                <li>No private control of the mass media: No private individual is allowed to own and publish newspaper, magazine, etc.</li>
                <li>One ideology: There is only one ideology for the country and it is for the party in government.</li>
                <li>Enforcement of government policies: The party in government ensures that all her policies and directives are strictly enforced.</li>
                <li>Opposition party: Opposition party is not legally recognised.</li>
                <li>Dictatorship: A one-party state can develop to become dictatorship.</li>
            </ol>
            
            <div class="comparison">
                <div class="system-card">
                    <h4>Merits of One-party System</h4>
                    <ul class="merits-list">
                        <li>Prevention of economic waste</li>
                        <li>It makes use of the best human resources</li>
                        <li>Promotion of unity</li>
                        <li>Stability of government</li>
                        <li>Makes for quick decisions</li>
                        <li>Continuity in government</li>
                        <li>Minimised electoral malpractice</li>
                        <li>Absence of unhealthy rivalry</li>
                        <li>It makes for strong leadership</li>
                    </ul>
                </div>
                
                <div class="system-card">
                    <h4>Demerits of One-party System</h4>
                    <ul class="demerits-list">
                        <li>No opposition</li>
                        <li>Individual rights are not accommodated</li>
                        <li>Control of mass media</li>
                        <li>It may produce dictatorship</li>
                        <li>It is basically undemocratic</li>
                        <li>It can produce "a president for life"</li>
                        <li>Only one ideology</li>
                        <li>Rule of law may not be respected</li>
                        <li>Change of government not provided for</li>
                    </ul>
                </div>
            </div>
            
            <div class="note">
                <p><strong>Difference between De jure and De facto One-party System</strong></p>
                <p><strong>De jure</strong> means only one party is legally and official recognised in the state while in <strong>de facto</strong>, other parties may exist, but only one party dominates the political scene.</p>
            </div>

        <section class="content-section two-party">
            <h2>9.5 TWO-PARTY SYSTEM</h2>
            <p><strong>Definition:</strong> A two-party system may be defined as the existence of only two major political parties in a political system. These two parties are constitutionally recognised to contest elections. Britain is a good example of a two-party state. There are two major political parties â€“ Labour and Conservative parties. Liberal party is a minor party. USA, Canada, Australia, etc. also practise a two-party system.</p>
            
            <h3>Features of Two-party System</h3>
            <ol>
                <li>Two major political parties: There are only two major political parties that are legally and constitutionally recognised. However, there may be in existence more than two political parties contesting elections.</li>
                <li>Opposition party: The opposition party is legally recognised in a two-party system.</li>
                <li>Choice: There is the opportunity for choice of candidates and parties.</li>
                <li>It is democratic: It accommodates democratic principles and allows the operation of the rule of law.</li>
                <li>Press freedom: A two-party system allows for press freedom, i.e. no censorship.</li>
                <li>Change of government: Change of government is possible because there is the provision for periodic elections.</li>
                <li>Corrective party: The opposition party is seen as a corrective party in government.</li>
            </ol>
            
            <div class="comparison">
                <div class="system-card">
                    <h4>Merits of Two-party System</h4>
                    <ul class="merits-list">
                        <li>Choice between political parties</li>
                        <li>Provision of strong opposition</li>
                        <li>It makes for good governance</li>
                        <li>It ensures stability</li>
                        <li>It is democratic</li>
                        <li>Easy identification of strengths and weaknesses</li>
                        <li>Change of government is possible</li>
                        <li>Difficult to degenerate into dictatorship</li>
                        <li>Promotes popular government</li>
                    </ul>
                </div>
                
                <div class="system-card">
                    <h4>Demerits of Two-party System</h4>
                    <ul class="demerits-list">
                        <li>Division of the country into two factions</li>
                        <li>It does not promote unity</li>
                        <li>It may lead to a one-party state</li>
                        <li>Electoral malpractices</li>
                        <li>Unhealthy party rivalry</li>
                        <li>Waste of the nation's resources</li>
                        <li>Can breed irresponsible opposition</li>
                        <li>May hinder development of minor parties</li>
                        <li>Party loyalty above national interest</li>
                        <li>May lead to victimisation</li>
                    </ul>
                </div>
            </div>
        </section>
        
        <section class="content-section multi-party">
            <h2>9.7 MULTI-PARTY SYSTEM</h2>
            <p><strong>Definition:</strong> It is defined as a system with more than two political parties are contesting for political power in a country. All the parties are duly registered and recognised by law. For example, Nigeria's Second Republic (1979-83) had six recognised political parties: NPN, UPN, NPP, GNPP, PRP and NAP. In a multi-party system, a coalition government may be formed, if no party had absolute majority seats in parliamentary election. However, coalition government is mostly common with parliamentary democracy. Nigeria, France, Italy, Zambia, Malawi, etc. practise multi-party systems.</p>
            
            <h3>Features of Multi-party System</h3>
            <ol>
                <li>Existence of many parties: There are more than two political parties in the system.</li>
                <li>Legal recognition: All the political parties are recognised by the law.</li>
                <li>Choice: Individuals can easily make choice of parties and programmes.</li>
                <li>Political education: The scope of political education is enhanced and widened.</li>
                <li>Wider representation: People of different interests and opinions are represented.</li>
                <li>Coalition government: May give rise to a coalition government.</li>
                <li>Absence of dictatorship: There is no place for dictatorship.</li>
                <li>Periodic election: Elections are conducted at periodic intervals.</li>
                <li>Protection of human rights: The rights of individuals are guaranteed.</li>
                <li>Respect for rule of law: There is respect for the rule of law.</li>
            </ol>
            
            <div class="comparison">
                <div class="system-card">
                    <h4>Merits of Multi-party System</h4>
                    <ul class="merits-list">
                        <li>It is democratic</li>
                        <li>Wider political participation and education</li>
                        <li>Wider representation</li>
                        <li>Reduction of single party dictatorship</li>
                        <li>Encourages better party organisation</li>
                        <li>Recognition of opposition parties</li>
                        <li>Change of government is possible</li>
                        <li>Human rights are protected</li>
                        <li>Respect for rule of law</li>
                        <li>Wide range of choices for electorate</li>
                        <li>Ensures political accountability</li>
                        <li>Addresses minority interest</li>
                        <li>Encourages healthy competition</li>
                        <li>Caters for all political opinions</li>
                        <li>Reflects political differences</li>
                    </ul>
                </div>
                
                <div class="system-card">
                    <h4>Demerits of Multi-party System</h4>
                    <ul class="demerits-list">
                        <li>Often leads to weak coalition governments</li>
                        <li>Too many political programmes can confuse people</li>
                        <li>Difficult to hold a particular party responsible</li>
                        <li>May produce ethnic and sectional parties</li>
                        <li>Makes formation of new government difficult</li>
                        <li>Very expensive party system</li>
                        <li>Victimisation of political opponents</li>
                        <li>Political instability</li>
                        <li>Weakens the executive</li>
                        <li>Can lower standard of public life</li>
                    </ul>
                </div>
            </div>
            
            <h3>Roles of Minority Parties in a Multi-party Democracy</h3>
            <ol>
                <li>They provide avenues for alternative government</li>
                <li>They project the interest of minority groups</li>
                <li>They help to promote unity and tolerance</li>
                <li>They keep the government accountable</li>
                <li>They help to prevent violation of Human Rights</li>
                <li>They offer useful suggestions for policies</li>
                <li>They contribute to parliamentary debates</li>
                <li>They serve on various parliamentary committees</li>
                <li>They assist in national consensus building</li>
                <li>They mobilise against unpopular policies</li>
                <li>They exhibit loyalty and patriotism in crises</li>
                <li>They protect the constitution and rule of law</li>
                <li>They provide alternative policies and programmes</li>
            </ol>
        </section>
        
        <section class="content-section zero-party">
            <h2>9.8 ZERO-PARTY SYSTEM</h2>
            <p><strong>Meaning:</strong> A zero-party system has to do with a standard where political leaders are elected by the people on an individual basis. There is no political party. Candidates are mostly independent and are elected according to their merits.</p>
            
            <div class="comparison">
                <div class="system-card">
                    <h4>Merits of Zero-party System</h4>
                    <ul class="merits-list">
                        <li>Proper management of time</li>
                        <li>Reduced electoral malpractices</li>
                        <li>Simple to operate</li>
                        <li>Reduced cost of elections</li>
                        <li>Easy choice for electorate</li>
                        <li>Quick and purposeful decision-making</li>
                        <li>Conservation of resources</li>
                    </ul>
                </div>
                
                <div class="system-card">
                    <h4>Demerits of Zero-party System</h4>
                    <ul class="demerits-list">
                        <li>Irresponsible political leaders</li>
                        <li>Poorly organised elections</li>
                        <li>Absence of party manifesto</li>
                        <li>Political instability</li>
                        <li>Absence of political education</li>
                    </ul>
                </div>
            </div>
        </section>
        
        <section class="content-section manifesto">
            <h2>9.9 PARTY MANIFESTO</h2>
            <p><strong>Definition:</strong> A party manifesto may be defined as the cardinal plans or programmes of a political party which the party intends to fulfil to the electorate if it is elected into power. It can also be referred to as the aims and objectives of a political party.</p>
            
            <h3>Importance or Merit of a Party Manifesto</h3>
            <ol>
                <li><strong>What to do and how to do it:</strong> It gives direction to the party's programmes of action and or ideology and stipulates how to achieve them.</li>
                <li><strong>Manifesto acts as magnetic pull:</strong> Party manifesto is usually a magnetic pull to the electorate, i.e. attract more members or supporters to the party.</li>
                <li><strong>It educates the electorate:</strong> Manifesto educates the electorate to have an insight into the aims and objectives and planned programmes of the political party.</li>
                <li><strong>Assessment:</strong> Party manifesto helps the electorate to assess the party on the basis of the contents of the manifesto.</li>
            </ol>
        </section>
