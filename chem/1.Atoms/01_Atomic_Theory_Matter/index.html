Topic 1.1

Atomic Theory of Matter

👨‍🔬
John Dalton (1766–1844)

British chemist and schoolteacher who proposed the first modern atomic theory. He was colorblind and studied it scientifically – that's why colorblindness is sometimes called "Daltonism"!

Dalton's big idea: All matter is made of tiny particles called atoms.

Before Dalton, people knew about elements and compounds, but no one understood why they behaved the way they did. Dalton provided the answer with his atomic theory – a set of simple rules that explained everything we observe about matter.

⚛️ The Four Postulates of Dalton's Atomic Theory

Here are the main points – think of them as the "rules of the game" for how matter works:

  • Postulate 1: The "Lego Brick" Rule All matter is made of tiny, indivisible particles called atoms. You can think of atoms like Lego bricks – the building blocks of everything around you.
  • Postulate 2: The "Same Type" Rule An element is made of only ONE kind of atom. All atoms of the same element are identical – they have the same mass and properties. (Kind of like all red Lego bricks are the same.)
  • Postulate 3: The "Recipe" Rule A compound is made of atoms of two or more elements combined in fixed proportions. It's like a recipe – water always has 2 hydrogen atoms for every 1 oxygen atom. You can't change the recipe and still call it water!
  • Postulate 4: The "Reorganization" Rule A chemical reaction is just a rearrangement of atoms. The atoms themselves are not created, destroyed, or broken apart – they just get reorganized into new combinations. Like taking apart a Lego castle and using the same bricks to build a spaceship.

🧪 In simpler terms: Atoms are the basic building blocks. Elements are made of one type of block. Compounds are made of different blocks stuck together in specific ways. Chemical reactions are just reorganizing the blocks.

🔤 Atomic Symbols – Shorthand for Elements

Chemists use atomic symbols as a kind of shorthand. It's way faster than writing the full element name every time!

  • One letter: Capital letter from the element's name (C = Carbon, O = Oxygen, H = Hydrogen)
  • Two letters: First letter capital, second lowercase (Cl = Chlorine, Ca = Calcium, Mg = Magnesium)
  • From Latin names: Some symbols come from ancient names. Sodium is Na (from natrium). Potassium is K (from kalium). Iron is Fe (from ferrum).

Dalton used to draw little spheres to represent atoms. Today we still use models, but they're much more detailed because we know more about what atoms actually look like inside!

🔍 What Dalton's Theory Explains

Dalton's theory wasn't just a wild guess – it explained things scientists had already observed:

⚖️ Law of Conservation of Mass

What it says: Mass stays the same during a chemical reaction. Nothing is lost, nothing is gained.

How Dalton explains it: Postulate 4 says atoms are just rearranged, not created or destroyed. Since each atom has mass, the total mass must stay the same. Simple!

📏 Law of Definite Proportions

What it says: A compound always has the same elements in the same proportions by mass. Water is always 11% hydrogen and 89% oxygen, no matter where you find it.

How Dalton explains it: Postulate 3 says compounds are made of atoms in fixed ratios. Fixed ratios = fixed proportions by mass.

💡 The Law That Proved Dalton Was Right

The best theories don't just explain old stuff – they predict new stuff. Dalton's theory predicted something that hadn't been noticed yet: the Law of Multiple Proportions.

🧪 The Carbon-Oxygen Example

Carbon and oxygen can form two different compounds:

  • Compound A: 1.000 g carbon + 1.332 g oxygen
  • Compound B: 1.000 g carbon + 2.664 g oxygen

Notice something? The oxygen in compound B is exactly twice the oxygen in compound A (2.664 ÷ 1.332 = 2).

Dalton's theory explains this perfectly:

  • Compound A = CO (1 carbon + 1 oxygen)
  • Compound B = CO₂ (1 carbon + 2 oxygen)

This was a huge deal! It convinced scientists that Dalton was onto something real.

⚠️ One Small Correction

Dalton got almost everything right, but we now know atoms can be split – they're made of even smaller particles (protons, neutrons, electrons). That's why in the next section, we'll update postulate 2 to match modern science. But for most chemical reactions, Dalton's theory works perfectly!
