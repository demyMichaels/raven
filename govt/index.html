Basic Concepts in Government

Power, Authority, Legitimacy, Sovereignty, and the Foundations of Political Organization

1.0 Introduction: Understanding Government

Government as an Institution: Government may be defined as a machinery established by the state to organise the state, manage its affairs and administer its functions and duties. It is also seen as a machinery through which the will of the state is formulated, expressed and realised.

The need for government arose as a result of people living together, interacting and working together as well as forming different associations. This living and working together may bring about conflicts, disagreements, crises of different dimensions. The fundamental function of any state is to come up with a dynamic policy to control people by the administration of justice so that the weak are protected from the strong and the poor from the wealthy.

Government as a Process or the Art of Governing

Government is referred to as an art of governing because it is a body vested with the supreme power of the affairs of a state. A government is vested with the power of maintaining peace and security by putting in place machinery for that purpose.

  • Government makes laws and enforces them on the people of that country.
  • It makes policies and implements them.
  • It interprets laws and punishes the offenders.

Government as an Academic Field of Study

Government as an academic field of study involves the study of political institutions in the state, ideas, values and doctrines about politics, the view of political thinkers on what constitutes the welfare of the people. It is a social science subject that is taught in schools and colleges as well as in higher institutions where it is popularly known as Political Science.

Branches of Government as an Academic Field:

  • Public Administration: Human management in government establishments or public affairs.
  • International Relations: Diplomacy and external relations with other countries.
  • Political Thought / Theories: Early political thinkers (Locke, Hobbes, Machiavelli).
  • Political Economy: Monetary policies of a country.
  • Comparative Politics: Comparative analysis of government of other countries.

Functions of Government

  • Law-making: Responsible for making laws to ensure peace and order.
  • Provision of social amenities: Electricity, potable water, health centres, etc.
  • Maintenance of law and order: Performed by agencies like the police.
  • Defence of the country: Protecting against external aggression.
  • Protection of lives and property: Citizens can seek police protection.
  • Provision of employment: Jobs provided for citizens.
  • Formulation and implementation of policies: Ensuring well-being of the people.
  • Promotion of economic activities: Through infrastructure and encouraging investors.
  • External affairs: Maintaining good relations with other countries.
  • Administration of justice: Settling disputes.
  • Maintenance of unity: Uniting ethnic groups and different beliefs.
  • Settlement of disputes: Between groups, individuals and government.
  • Economic functions: Ensuring economic growth, regulating the economy.
  • Political functions: Conducting regular, free and fair elections.
  • Raising of public revenue: Through taxes, fines, fees, duties.
  • Fundamental human rights: Protecting rights entrenched in the constitution.
  • Revenue generation: For infrastructure and social amenities.
  • Protection of national interest: In pursuing foreign policy objectives.
  • Conduct of elections and population census: Through agencies like INEC.

Characteristics of Government

  • Law: Guide to activities, beliefs and behaviour of citizens.
  • Political power: In consonance with the people's will.
  • Revenue: Needed for the enormous task facing government.
  • Work-force: Civil service with adequate personnel.
  • People's legitimate support: Produces peace and stability.
  • Welfare services: Provision of essential services.
  • Periodic change: No government is permanent.
  • Protection: Providing security to the people.

1.1 Power, Authority, Legitimacy & Sovereignty

POWER

Definition: Power is the capacity to affect another's behaviour by the threat of sanction. The sanctions may be negative or positive. Thus, a political leader may have the ability to control the actions of others by promising those who support him wealth or honours, or he may threaten to deny such rewards to those who oppose him.

Forms of Power

  • Political power: The elected President or Prime Minister possesses political power derived from the people and the constitution.
  • Economic power: An individual in possession of economic resources could use such to influence issues in a state.
  • Military power: The armed forces control the instruments of violence in a country.
  • Physical power: The application of force in compelling obedience.

The Acquisition and Exercise of Power

  • The constitution: Specifies how powers should be acquired and exercised.
  • Through coercion: Power may be acquired through the use of force, e.g. the military.
  • Through charisma: Power can be acquired through intelligence or a person's charisma.
  • Through economic resources: Money and property can help acquire political power.
  • Through position of authority: Authority and power are complementary.
  • Through inheritance: Based on heredity, mostly with royal families.

AUTHORITY

Definition: Authority refers to the formal or legal right to make and enforce laws or policies which the citizens obey. Authority connotes the acceptance of the right to rule as conferred by the people, irrespective of the sanctions.

Kinds of Authority

  • Institutional authority: A group of individuals who legitimately occupies Public Office with capacity to determine public goals and policies.
  • Functional authority: Power which accrues to a group of people or an individual as a result of the position he holds in an organisation.

Sources of Political Authority

  • Traditional authority: Derivable from customs and traditions of the people.
  • Legal authority: Source is mostly the constitution of a given country.
  • Charismatic authority: Derived from special and unique qualities of an individual.
  • Consent of the people: People may decide to invest a certain person with authority.

Types of Authority

  • Traditional authority: Found in simple traditional societies.
  • Charismatic authority: Derived from belief in exceptional ability of a leader.
  • Legal/Rational authority: Derived from the constitution.
  • Delegated authority: Given to someone to exercise functions on specific areas.
  • Technical authority: Based on level of expertise.
  • Political authority: Conferred on persons holding political offices.
  • Positional authority: Determined by the position an individual holds.
  • Coercive authority: Based on the use of force.

De-jure and De-facto Authority

  • De-jure Authority: The right to issue instructions and to have them obeyed. When an official exceeds the limits of rules, such act would be regarded as "Ultra Vires".
  • De-facto Authority: A situation where one person acknowledges another as being entitled to command him.

Differences between Power and Authority

Power Authority
Capacity to affect behaviour by threat of sanction Formal or legal right to make and enforce laws
Can be legitimate or illegitimate Based on acceptance and mostly legitimate
Cannot be delegated Can be delegated with legitimised responsibility
Punishment is more serious Punishment is not as serious as that of power

LEGITIMACY

Definition: Legitimacy is a political concept which refers to the acceptance and recognition by the citizens of a country, the rights of their rulers to govern. A government is considered legitimate if it comes to power through constitutional means.

Features of Legitimacy

  • Legality: Government comes to power through lawful means.
  • Acceptability: Acceptable by the people.
  • Recognition: Recognised both internally and externally.
  • Popularity: Receives popular support from the people.
  • Accountability and transparency: Open in all its activities.
  • Satisfaction of the needs of the people.
  • Periodic free and fair elections.
  • Constitutionalism: Adherence to provisions of the constitution.
  • Mechanism to check arbitrariness.
  • Responsiveness to people's views.
  • Respect for rules, customs and traditions.
  • Respect for fundamental human rights.
  • Existence of rule of law.
  • Existence of free press.
  • Popular participation.

Factors that Determine Legitimacy

  • Good/Reliable leadership
  • Good government policies
  • Periodic free and fair elections
  • Respect for customs and traditions
  • Foreign policy objectives
  • Wider scope of political participation
  • Ideology
  • Charisma of leaders
  • Popular support
  • Open-door administration
  • Respect for human rights
  • Socio-economic development
  • Adherence to constitutional provisions

SOVEREIGNTY

Definition: Sovereignty means the supreme power and authority of the state over the citizens. It is the power of the state to make laws and enforce them with all the means of coercion it cares to employ, without any interference by other states.

The idea of sovereignty originated from Jean Bodin (1530-1596), a French political thinker and philosopher.

Principal Characteristics of Sovereignty

  • Indivisibility: Sovereignty cannot be divided, though government can delegate powers.
  • Absoluteness: The powers of a sovereign state cannot be restricted.
  • Permanence: As long as the state exists, sovereignty continues without interruption.
  • Independence of foreign control: A sovereign state is independent of foreign control.
  • Comprehensiveness: The powers exercised are wide and encompassing.

Types of Sovereignty

  • Legal sovereignty: The body that makes laws and enforces them.
  • Political or popular sovereignty: The body whose will is ultimately obeyed (the electorate).
  • De facto sovereignty: The body or group that uses force to make citizens obey after overthrowing the legitimate sovereign.
  • De jure sovereignty: Sovereign rule based on law and its application.
  • Internal sovereignty: Supreme power to make and enforce laws within its area.
  • External sovereignty: Power to exist and conduct affairs without external interference.

Location of Sovereignty in a State

  • In a federal state: Sovereignty is shared since powers are constitutionally shared.
  • In a unitary state: Sovereignty resides with the central government.
  • The electorate: Political sovereignty resides with the electorate.
  • In a monarch: Can be traced to the Queen in Parliament (Britain).
  • In a military regime: Sovereignty resides in the central decision-making body.

Limitations to the Sovereignty of a State

Internal Limitations:

  • The constitution
  • The electorate
  • Public opinion
  • The Military
  • Customs and traditions
  • Religion and culture
  • Delegated legislation
  • Resources (human and material)
  • Activities of pressure groups
  • Type of government in power

External Limitations:

  • International organisations
  • International laws and conventions
  • Inter-dependence of states
  • Dominance of powerful states
  • Military agreements or pacts
  • External aids
  • International financial institutions
  • Diplomatic immunity
  • External trade
  • Geographical location

1.2 Society, State, Nation & Nation-State

Understanding the foundations of political organization, sovereignty, and the relationship between state, government, and society

The State

Definition: A state may be defined as a politically organised body of people inhabiting a defined geographical entity with an organised legitimate government. The state is entirely free from external control. The power of a state is guaranteed with coercive power to secure obedience from the citizens.

Important Distinction: A state is different from empires, kingdoms, and chieftains. The modern state is characterized by defined boundaries, sovereign authority, and organized government.

Relationship between State and Government

The state and government are inseparable. The state is a politically organised body of people occupying a defined geographical area with an organised legitimate government. Government is a feature of the state—a machinery set up by the state to organise and realise its set objectives and goals.

The State The Government
Permanent entity Changeable institution
Corporate body Administrative machinery
Holds sovereignty Exercises sovereignty in trust
Comprises people and territory Has three organs (Executive, Legislative, Judicial)

Key Point: No state exists without a government, otherwise anarchy would reign. Government can change many times but the state remains a permanent feature.

State and Nation

  • State: Exists where a territory, a people, a government, and sovereignty exist. It may lack the feeling of nationality or oneness among the people and yet remain a state. The state may accommodate people of different historical backgrounds, languages, cultures, and religious beliefs.
  • Nation: A body of people who feel themselves to be naturally linked together, share common ties, and believe they can live happily together. They share the same language, culture, and have the feeling of nationality.

Lord Acton's Perspective: "The combination of different nations in one state is as necessary a condition of civilised life as the combination of individuals to form society."

State and Society

Society is referred to as the association of human beings. This suggests the whole complex of relations of man to his fellows. Society can equally be referred to as a group of people who share similar values, laws, traditions, and live in organised communities for mutual benefits.

Features of a Society

  • Controlled by rules, regulations, norms, conventions, and customs
  • Applies sanctions to ensure discipline
  • Uses coercive means to ensure compliance
  • Rules are binding on all residents
  • Membership is voluntary
  • Permanent in nature
  • Members depend on one another for survival
  • Roles are dynamic
  • Characterised by cooperation, competition, and conflicts
  • Characterised by unique culture
  • Common political, social, and economic interests
  • Common history, tradition, language, religion, and sense of value

Differences between State and Society

  1. Scope: Society is a wider term than the state. It suggests many social relationships which cannot be expressed through the state (education, religion, agricultural and industrial activities). The state is only concerned with those social relationships that express themselves through government.
  2. Organisation: Society applies to all human communities whether organised or unorganised, but organisation for law is essential to a state.
  3. Identity: State is not identical with society. The state provides the framework of the social order, holds society together, and binds individuals to certain uniform rules of behaviour essential for a harmonious and orderly social life.

1.3 Political Processes: Socialization, Participation & Culture

POLITICAL CULTURE

Definition: Political culture refers to the way the members of a political community behave in their political activities, e.g. voting. It refers to the usual attitude, beliefs and perception of people towards politics. It also deals with the various reasons which conditioned the behaviour of the people in a political community.

Some countries with developed and established political cultures are Britain, USA and France.

Components of Political Culture

  • Cognitive orientation: The beliefs of the people about the political system involving the government and their roles.
  • Evaluative orientation: The extent to which people can evaluate or assess the activities of those in power.
  • Affective orientation: The feelings, support and loyalty of the people towards the activities and performance of the government.

Determinants of Political Culture

  • Historical development: The French Revolution of 1789 violently overthrew existing political structures.
  • Impact of European colonial domination: Different effects of British and French control in West Africa.
  • Ethnic differences: Impact on political culture varies; has long affected attitude in Nigeria.
  • Instability: Caused by ethnic groups directing loyalties to themselves rather than national government.
  • Socio-economic structure: Urban, industrialised societies differ from rural, conservative societies.
  • Industrialised society: Complex class structure affects political culture.

POLITICAL SOCIALISATION

Definition: Political socialisation is defined as the process of acquiring political beliefs, values, attitude as well as citizenship training that shapes a citizen's political behaviour. It is also the process of political learning from childhood to adulthood. It is a life-long developmental process.

Agents of Political Socialisation

  • Family unit: Transmission of knowledge about politics, authority and allocation of values starts at home.
  • The peer group: The group one moves with helps to dictate or influence one's political emotion.
  • Institutions of learning: Schools, colleges provide avenue for political talks, debates and discussions.
  • Social/Religious institutions: Members engage in political arguments and discussions.
  • The mass media: Radio, TV, newspapers disseminate political news and analyse political issues.
  • Political parties: Educate members through manifestoes, campaigns, rallies.
  • Pressure groups: Educate members through conferences, seminars on political and civic rights.

Political Obligation

Authority gives rulers the right to command while political obligation deals with why people obey government.

Theories of Obligation

  • The theory of coercion: Men obey government because they recognise their incapacity in relation to government's coercive force.
  • Prudential obligation: Men obey government because they value individual freedom and conveniences more than the pains of disobedience. (Thomas Hobbes)
  • The liberal theory of obligation: Men obey government because they are in the habit of obedience; a by-product of the socialisation process.
  • The social contract theory: Political obligation derives from the relationship between ruler and ruled created by a contract. (Locke)
  • The theory of the common good: Rulers pursue moral ends in the interest of all.
  • The theory of the general will: Societies have a general will of purpose which they pursue, in the interest of all. (Plato and Hegel)

POLITICAL PARTICIPATION

Definition: Political participation is defined as the process whereby individuals get themselves involved in the political activities and in the decision-making of their country.

Forms of Political Participation

  • Voting in elections
  • Holding of public offices
  • Contesting elections
  • Supporting political parties
  • Belonging to a political party
  • Supporting candidates for elective posts
  • Protests and demonstration
  • Partial political participation (attending campaigns, rallies)
  • Observatory political participation (listening to political talks, discussions)

The Purpose of Political Participation

  • To contribute to decision-making
  • Because past governments have failed the people
  • To correct ills in society (bribery, corruption, tribalism)
  • For money-making and gains
  • For respect and prestige
  • As an occupation
  • To be involved in managing the nation's resources equitably

Factors Affecting Political Participation

  • Lack of trust in government: If previous governments failed, people may lose confidence.
  • Fear of intimidation: May deny individual opportunity to express opinion freely.
  • Apathy or lack of interest: Individual sees politics as not relevant.
  • Ignorance or illiteracy: May not see the need to participate.
  • Frequent removal of elected governments: Military intervention can work against participation.
  • Poverty: Politics requires money; poverty may deny opportunity.
