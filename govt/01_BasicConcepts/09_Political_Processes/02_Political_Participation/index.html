Topic 1.10

Political Participation

📜
John Stuart Mill (1806–1873)

British philosopher who argued that participation in politics is essential for human development. He believed that voting and engaging in public affairs makes us better, more thoughtful citizens.

His key idea: "The only way to become a good citizen is to participate in the business of government."

Have you ever voted in a school election? Signed a petition? Attended a protest? Even talked about politics with friends? That's political participation – any activity that shapes government decisions or helps choose leaders.

Definition of Political Participation: Political participation is the process whereby individuals get involved in political activities and decision-making in their country. It's how citizens make their voices heard.

Think of political participation like being part of a team. On a football team, you can be the captain, a player, a substitute, or even just a fan cheering from the stands. All are forms of participation. In politics, you can vote, campaign, protest, or just follow the news – you're still part of the game.

🗳️ Forms of Political Participation (Ways to Get Involved)

There are many ways citizens can participate – from the very active to the more casual:

🗳️
Voting

The most common form – choosing leaders in elections

📢
Protesting

Public demonstrations to show support or opposition

✍️
Petitions

Signing or starting a petition for change

🎯
Joining a Party

Becoming a member of a political party

🏃
Running for Office

Standing as a candidate in elections

💬
Discussing Politics

Talking with friends, family, or online

📰
Following News

Staying informed about political issues

💰
Donating

Giving money to a candidate or cause

  • 🗳️ 1. Voting in elections This is the most basic and widespread form. By voting, citizens choose who represents them and which policies to support.
  • 🏛️ 2. Holding public office Serving as an elected or appointed official allows direct decision-making power.
  • 🏃 3. Contesting elections Standing as a candidate for an elective post – a more intense form of participation.
  • 🎯 4. Supporting political parties Working for a party, campaigning, or even just wearing a party badge.
  • 👥 5. Belonging to a political party Active and direct involvement in party activities, congresses, and campaigns.
  • 🤝 6. Supporting candidates for elective posts Moral or financial support for a candidate you believe in.
  • 📢 7. Protests and demonstrations Taking to the streets to show support for or against policies – a powerful form of voice.
  • 👀 8. Partial political participation Attending rallies, listening to debates, or following political news – less active but still engaged.
  • 👂 9. Observatory political participation Simply taking interest in political discussions, talks, and media – the first step toward involvement.

🎯 Why Do People Participate? (The Purpose)

People get involved in politics for many reasons:

  • To influence decisions: They want a say in how their country is run.
  • Because past governments failed: They want to fix problems like bad roads, corruption, or lack of schools.
  • To correct ills in society: Issues like tribalism, bribery, or injustice motivate people to act.
  • For personal gain: Some see politics as a way to make money or gain respect.
  • As a career: Politics can be a chosen profession.
  • To manage national resources: Skilled individuals want to ensure resources are used wisely.
  • Because they believe in democracy: They see participation as a civic duty.

🚫 What Stops People from Participating? (Factors Affecting Participation)

Not everyone participates equally. Here are common barriers:

  • 😞 Lack of trust in government If previous governments broke promises, people may feel "why bother?"
  • 😨 Fear of intimidation In some places, speaking out can be dangerous – that stops participation.
  • 😐 Apathy or lack of interest Some people just don't care about politics – they see it as irrelevant to their lives.
  • 📚 Ignorance or illiteracy Without education, people may not know how to participate or why it matters.
  • ⚔️ Frequent removal of governments Military coups and instability can make people feel that participation is useless.
  • 💵 Poverty "Politics is money" – if you're struggling to survive, you may not have time or resources for politics.

🇳🇬 Political Participation in Nigeria – Real Examples

🗳️ Voting in Elections

Every four years, Nigerians queue to vote for president, governors, and legislators. Turnout varies – in 2019, about 35% of registered voters participated. Why so low? Apathy, distrust, and logistical challenges play a role.

📢 #EndSARS Protests (2020)

Young Nigerians took to the streets to demand police reform. This was massive political participation – not through voting, but through protest. It showed that when people are passionate, they will participate.

🎯 Party Membership

During election seasons, millions attend rallies for APC, PDP, and other parties. Wearing party shirts, waving flags, and cheering – that's participation too.

👥 Community Meetings

In many villages, town hall meetings discuss local issues – from building a well to settling disputes. That's political participation at the grassroots.

📖 A Story: Aisha's First Vote

"Finally, My Voice Counts" – Aisha turned 18 just before the 2023 elections. She'd heard her parents complain about politicians for years. But she also saw the #EndSARS protests and realized that young people could make a difference.

She registered to vote, researched the candidates, and on election day, she joined the long queue under the sun. When she finally pressed her thumbprint on the ballot paper, she felt proud. "This is my chance to shape my future," she thought.

Afterwards, she posted on social media urging her friends to vote. Some did, some didn't. But Aisha learned that participation isn't just about one vote – it's about being part of something bigger.

To sum up: Political participation is how citizens engage with their government – from voting to protesting to just staying informed. Many factors encourage or discourage participation: trust, education, fear, poverty. In a democracy, participation is what makes government "by the people" real. Whether you vote, protest, or simply discuss, you're part of the process.
