Topic 1.9

Political Socialization

Charles Cooley (1864–1929)

American sociologist who developed the idea of the "looking-glass self" – we learn who we are by seeing ourselves through others' eyes. This is exactly how political socialization works.

His key idea: "We learn our values, including political ones, from the people around us."

Charles Cooley: Looking-Glass Self (Khan Academy)

A clear explanation of how we form our identity based on how we think others see us – the foundation of political socialization.

Have you ever noticed how you picked up your parents' opinions about politics? Or how your friends influence what you think is fair? That's political socialization in action – it's how we learn to become political beings.

Definition of Political Socialization: Political socialization is the process of acquiring political beliefs, values, attitudes, and behaviors. It's how we learn about politics from childhood through adulthood – a lifelong journey of political learning.

Think of political socialization like learning a language. As a baby, you picked up your first words from family. In school, you learned more formal grammar. From friends, you picked up slang. Throughout life, you keep learning new words and ways of speaking. Politics is the same – you learn it bit by bit from everyone around you.

  • 👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 1. Family This is where it all begins. Around the dinner table, kids hear parents discuss politics, complain about taxes, or praise a leader. Even if parents don't realize it, they're teaching political values.
    🏫 School analogy: Like learning your first words from parents – family gives you your political "first words" too.
  • 🏫 2. School In class, you learn about government, history, and citizenship. Morning assemblies, pledge of allegiance, civic education – all shape how you see your country.
  • 👥 3. Peer Group As you grow, friends influence you. If your friends are passionate about a cause, you might become passionate too. Peer pressure works politically as well.
  • ⛪ 4. Religious Institutions Churches, mosques, and temples often teach values that have political meaning – like caring for the poor, justice, or community responsibility.
  • 📺 5. Mass Media TV news, newspapers, TikTok, Instagram – media shapes what we think about and how we think about it. If a issue is all over the news, we care about it. If it's ignored, we might not even know it exists.
  • 🎯 6. Political Parties Parties educate through manifestos, rallies, and campaigns. They try to convince you their way is best.
  • 📢 7. Pressure Groups Groups like labour unions, environmental activists, or human rights organizations influence our views on specific issues.

👥 Family vs. Peers – A Quick Comparison

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Family

  • First influence (ages 0–10)
  • Shapes basic values (e.g., "we support this party")
  • Long-lasting, but can be questioned later

👥 Peers

  • Strong influence as teens
  • Shapes current opinions (e.g., "this protest matters")
  • Can challenge or change family views

🇳🇬 Political Socialization in Nigeria – Real Examples

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Nigerian Family

In many Nigerian homes, parents openly discuss politics, and children absorb those views. A child whose father is a staunch supporter of a particular party often grows up supporting that same party – at least until they encounter other influences.

⛪ Religious Institutions

In Nigeria, churches and mosques are powerful political influencers. Pastors and imams may endorse candidates or speak on issues like corruption, poverty, or Sharia law. Their words shape how millions of Nigerians think about politics.

📺 Mass Media

Nigerian media – from NTA to Channels TV, from social media influencers to Nollywood – constantly sends political messages. A popular musician's song about bad leadership can influence young people more than a formal political speech.

🎯 Political Parties

During elections, parties like APC and PDP hold rallies, hand out merchandise, and run ads. These activities socialize citizens into supporting certain ideologies or personalities.

📖 A Story: Chidi's Political Journey

"From Dinner Table to Ballot Box" – When Chidi was 8, he heard his father complain about potholes in their street. "The government doesn't care about us," his father said. Chidi didn't really understand, but he stored it away.

At 12, his teacher explained how local government works. Chidi learned that the potholes were the council's responsibility. In secondary school, his friends argued passionately about which party would fix the roads. Some supported APC, others PDP.

On social media, Chidi saw videos of politicians making promises, and memes mocking those who broke them. By the time he turned 18, Chidi had formed his own opinions – shaped by family, school, friends, and media. He was ready to vote.

That's political socialization – a lifetime of learning that turns us into political citizens.

🌟 Why Political Socialization Matters

  • It creates citizens: Without it, we wouldn't know how to participate in politics.
  • It passes on values: Each generation teaches the next what matters.
  • It can also change things: Young people often challenge old ideas – that's how societies evolve.
  • It explains differences: Why do some people vote one way and others another? Look at their socialization.

To sum up: Political socialization is how we learn about politics – from family, school, friends, media, religion, and parties. It starts in childhood and never really stops. Understanding it helps us see why people believe what they believe, and how political cultures are passed from one generation to the next.
