Topic 1.11

Political Culture

🌍
Gabriel Almond (1911–2002)

American political scientist who first developed the concept of political culture. He showed that every country has a unique "political personality" – shared attitudes that shape how government works.

His key idea: "Political culture is the pattern of individual attitudes and orientations toward politics."

Have you noticed how people in different countries have different feelings about government? In some places, people trust their leaders. In others, they're suspicious. Some citizens feel they can change things; others feel powerless. These shared feelings are political culture.

Definition of Political Culture: Political culture is the set of attitudes, beliefs, and sentiments that give order and meaning to a political process. It's the "political personality" of a society – how people think and feel about politics, government, and their role in it.

Think of political culture like the "vibe" of a school. Some schools have a culture where students participate, challenge teachers respectfully, and feel their voice matters. Other schools have a culture where students just follow rules and never speak up. Both schools have rules, but the feeling is totally different. That's culture – and countries are the same.

🧩 Components of Political Culture (The Three Parts)

Political scientists break political culture into three parts – how we think, feel, and judge politics:

🧠
Cognitive

What we KNOW about politics

❤️
Affective

How we FEEL about politics

⚖️
Evaluative

How we JUDGE politics

  • 🧠 1. Cognitive Orientation (What we KNOW) This is about knowledge and beliefs. Do citizens know how their government works? Do they understand elections, parties, and laws? In some countries, civic education is strong – people know their rights and how to vote. In others, knowledge is low.
    🏫 School analogy: Do students know the school rules? Do they know how to appeal a decision? That's cognitive orientation.
  • ❤️ 2. Affective Orientation (How we FEEL) This is about emotions – loyalty, pride, anger, trust. Do citizens feel proud of their country? Do they trust politicians? Do they feel connected to their nation?
    🏫 School analogy: Do students feel school spirit? Do they trust the principal? That's affective orientation.
  • ⚖️ 3. Evaluative Orientation (How we JUDGE) This is about making judgments. Is the government doing a good job? Are policies fair? Citizens constantly evaluate their leaders and systems.
    🏫 School analogy: Do students think the teachers are fair? Is the new uniform policy a good idea? That's evaluation.

🌍 Examples of Different Political Cultures

🇬🇧 Britain – Deferential Culture

British political culture has long been described as "deferential" – citizens tend to respect authority and trust leaders to make decisions. People participate, but they also accept that elites know best.

🇺🇸 USA – Participatory Culture

Americans are known for believing they can change things. "We the people" is central. Citizens feel it's their right – and duty – to speak out, protest, and challenge authority.

🇫🇷 France – Revolutionary Culture

French political culture includes a tradition of protest and revolution. Citizens are quick to take to the streets when they disagree. The culture expects conflict as part of politics.

📊 Determinants of Political Culture (What Shapes It?)

Why do countries develop different political cultures? Here are key factors:

  • 📜 Historical development: Events like wars, revolutions, or independence shape how people see government. The French Revolution still influences French attitudes today.
  • 🏛️ Colonial experience: Countries colonized by Britain (like Nigeria) inherited different political traditions than those colonized by France. British indirect rule vs. French assimilation – these left marks.
  • 👥 Ethnic differences: In multi-ethnic societies, groups may have different political cultures. In Nigeria, political attitudes can vary between North and South.
  • ⚖️ Instability: Countries with frequent coups or civil wars develop cultures of suspicion and distrust.
  • 🏭 Socio-economic structure: Industrialized, urban societies tend to have more participatory cultures. Rural, traditional societies may be more deferential.
  • 📚 Education: Higher education levels generally lead to more critical, engaged citizens.
  • ⛪ Religion: Religious traditions shape values about authority, community, and justice.

🇳🇬 Political Culture in Nigeria – Real Examples

🧠 Cognitive: What Nigerians Know

During elections, INEC runs voter education campaigns. But many Nigerians still don't fully understand the political system. Civic education in schools varies, so knowledge levels differ.

❤️ Affective: How Nigerians Feel

There's a mix: strong patriotism (especially during football matches!) but also deep distrust of politicians. Many feel the government doesn't care about them – which affects participation.

⚖️ Evaluative: Judging Government

Nigerians constantly evaluate: Is the government delivering? Are roads being built? Is corruption getting better? These judgments shape whether people vote or protest.

📜 Historical Influence

Military rule (1966–1979, 1983–1998) created a culture of distrust. People learned that governments could be overthrown – that affects attitudes today.

👥 Ethnic Differences

Political attitudes can vary. In some areas, traditional chiefs still hold great influence – people may trust them more than elected officials.

📖 A Story: Two Classrooms, Two Cultures

"Different Vibes, Different Schools" – In School A, students freely ask questions. They challenge the teacher (politely!). They have a student council that actually makes decisions. The principal holds town halls. Students feel they belong.

In School B, students are silent. They never question. The teacher gives orders, and they obey. The student council is just for show. Students feel powerless – they just wait to graduate.

Both are schools. Both have rules. But their cultures are totally different. Countries are the same – their political culture shapes whether citizens participate, trust, or just go along.

🌟 Why Political Culture Matters

  • It explains differences: Why do some democracies work and others fail? Often, it's culture.
  • It shapes participation: In participatory cultures, people vote and protest. In passive cultures, they don't.
  • It affects stability: When culture supports democracy (trust, tolerance), democracy lasts. When it doesn't, instability follows.
  • It changes slowly: Political culture takes generations to shift – understanding it helps predict the future.

To sum up: Political culture is the "personality" of a nation's politics – what people know, feel, and judge about government. It's shaped by history, colonialism, ethnicity, and education. Britain, the US, and France all have distinct political cultures. In Nigeria, our culture is shaped by military rule, ethnic diversity, and a mix of trust and distrust. Understanding political culture helps us see why citizens act the way they do – and why change is sometimes slow.
