Topic 1.3

Legitimacy

John Locke (1632–1704)

English philosopher who argued that government only has the right to rule if people CONSENT to it. If a government breaks the trust, people have the right to replace it.

His key idea: "Government's legitimacy comes from the consent of the governed."

Ever had a group project where someone just started bossing everyone around without being chosen? Annoying, right? That's a government without legitimacy. Legitimacy is what makes authority feel RIGHT – it's why we obey even when no one's watching.

Legitimacy (simple definition): The acceptance and recognition by citizens that their rulers have the RIGHT to govern. A legitimate government is one people believe in, not just fear.

Think of a substitute teacher vs. your regular teacher. The sub has legal authority (the school hired them), but if students don't respect them, they lack legitimacy. Your regular teacher? Students accept their rules because they've earned trust over time – that's legitimacy.

✅ Features of a Legitimate Government (How to Spot One)

A government isn't legitimate just because it SAYS it is. Here's what real legitimacy looks like:

  • Legality – Came to power through lawful means (elections, constitutional succession).
  • Acceptability – The people willingly accept it as their government.
  • Recognition – Recognized both internally (by citizens) and externally (by other countries).
  • Popularity – Has genuine support from the population.
  • Accountability & Transparency – Open about its actions and answers to the people.
  • Free & Fair Elections – Periodic elections where people truly choose.
  • Constitutionalism – Follows the rules in the constitution.
  • Rule of Law – Everyone, including leaders, obeys the law.
  • Respect for Human Rights – Protects citizens' fundamental rights.
  • Free Press – Media can criticize without fear.
  • Popular Participation – Citizens willingly take part in politics (voting, rallies, etc.).

Free and fair elections are a key sign of legitimacy.

📊 What Makes People Accept a Government? (13 Factors)

Legitimacy isn't automatic – governments have to EARN it. Here's what makes citizens say "yes, this government is right for us":

  • Good leadership: Leaders who perform well earn trust.
  • Good government policies: When policies actually help people.
  • Free and fair elections: People feel they chose the government.
  • Respect for customs and traditions: Honoring the culture of the people.
  • Charisma of leaders: Inspiring figures like Mandela.
  • Popular support: Mass participation shows acceptance.
  • Open-door administration: Listening to criticism and feedback.
  • Respect for human rights: Protecting citizens from abuse.
  • Socio-economic development: Building roads, schools, hospitals.
  • Adherence to the constitution: Following the rules.
  • Foreign policy success: Good international standing.
  • Inclusive governance: All groups feel represented.
  • Ideological consistency: Sticking to stated principles.

⚖️ Two Kinds of Legitimacy

De-jure Legitimacy

Legal legitimacy – based on law and constitution. The government came to power through proper legal channels.

Example: An elected president who follows the constitution.

De-facto Legitimacy

Actual legitimacy – people accept and obey, even if the government came to power unconstitutionally.

Example: A military government that gains popular support through good policies.

In school: A prefect elected by students has de-jure legitimacy (legal process). But if a student informally becomes the person everyone listens to for conflict resolution, they have de-facto legitimacy.

⚔️ Can Military Governments Be Legitimate?

This is a tricky question. By definition, military governments take power by force – so they lack de-jure legitimacy. But some argue they can gain de-facto legitimacy if:

  • They restore order after chaos
  • They provide good governance
  • People accept them willingly
  • They eventually hand over power to civilians

Example: Some Nigerians accepted military regimes in the past when they built infrastructure and maintained stability – but many also rejected them because they weren't elected.

🗣️ Legitimacy in Your Daily Life

  • Class prefect: If elected fairly, they have legitimacy. If appointed by a teacher without student input, less so.
  • Group project leader: The one everyone agrees on? Legitimate. The one who just starts assigning work? Not legitimate – that's just power.
  • Parental rules: When parents explain why a rule exists, it feels more legitimate than arbitrary "because I said so."
  • School rules: Rules that make sense (no phones in exams) feel legitimate. Rules that seem pointless? Students find ways around them.

🇳🇬 Legitimacy in Nigeria

  • Civilian government (1999–present): De-jure legitimacy through elections, though some elections were disputed – affecting perceived legitimacy.
  • Military regimes (1966–1979, 1983–1998): Lacked de-jure legitimacy, but some (like Murtala Mohammed) gained popular acceptance through policies.
  • Traditional rulers: The Oba of Benin has legitimacy through tradition and custom, not elections.
  • INEC (electoral commission): Its legitimacy depends on whether people trust it to conduct fair elections.

🔴 REAL NEWS: #EndSARS Protests (October 2020) – When a Government Loses Legitimacy

📍 Nigeria | October 2020

In October 2020, thousands of young Nigerians took to the streets under the #EndSARS movement. They demanded the disbandment of the Special Anti-Robbery Squad (SARS), a police unit accused of brutality, extortion, and extrajudicial killings.

Why did this happen? The police – a key institution of government – had lost legitimacy in the eyes of many citizens. People no longer believed the police would protect them; instead, they feared them. Despite having legal authority, the police's actions made them illegitimate in the public's view.

What happened next? The government initially promised reforms, but when protests continued, security forces cracked down. At the Lekki toll gate on October 20, 2020, soldiers allegedly opened fire on peaceful protesters. This further eroded trust.

The lesson: Even an elected government can lose legitimacy if its institutions abuse power. When legitimacy collapses, people resist – through protests, refusal to cooperate, or even rebellion. As John Locke said, when trust is broken, people have the right to withdraw their consent.

#EndSARS shows that legitimacy isn't permanent – it must be earned daily through justice, accountability, and respect for human rights.

📖 Legitimacy in Action – The Unpopular Principal

"The New Principal's Challenge" – When Mrs. Okonkwo became principal, she had full legal authority. But she started changing rules without explanation, ignored student concerns, and played favorites with teachers. Soon, students started sneaking out, teachers complained, and even parents questioned her. She had power, but she lost legitimacy. Contrast with Mr. Adebayo, who held town halls, explained his decisions, and listened. Same legal authority – but Mr. Adebayo kept his legitimacy because people believed in him.

🌟 Why Should We Care About Legitimacy?

  • Stability: Legitimate governments don't need to use force to make people obey.
  • Cooperation: People pay taxes, follow laws, and participate willingly.
  • International respect: Other countries deal with legitimate governments.
  • Peaceful transitions: When governments lose legitimacy, people protest or rebel.
