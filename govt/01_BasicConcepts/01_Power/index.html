Topic 1.1

Power

🎭
Niccolò Machiavelli (1469–1527)

Italian writer who studied power like a scientist. He noticed that leaders often had to make tough choices to stay in control.

His famous quote: "It's better to be feared than loved if you can't be both." Harsh, but it shows how power can work.

🎥 Who was Machiavelli? (The School of Life)

A fair and engaging introduction to the man behind the myth – why he wasn't just about "evil" scheming.

Ever wonder why some people just seem to get their way? That's power in action. In government, power is the engine that makes things happen – for better or worse.

Definition: Power is the capacity to affect another's behaviour by the threat of sanction. Sanctions may be negative (punishment) or positive (rewards). A political leader, for example, can control others by promising wealth or honours, or by threatening to deny them.

Think of power like the remote control in a group of friends. Whoever holds the remote decides what everyone watches. In government, the "remote" decides who gets what, when, and how.

⚡ Forms of Power

  • Political Power The power of elected leaders – presidents, governors, members of parliament.
    🎮 Analogy: The person who gets to be team captain in gym class. They decide positions, substitutions, and strategy.
  • Economic Power Control over money and resources.
    🎮 Analogy: The friend who always has snacks. They might not be team captain, but everyone listens when they say where to go after school.
  • Military Power Control over armed forces – army, navy, air force.
    🎮 Analogy: The school security guard. They have the authority to step in if things get out of hand.
  • Physical Power Direct force – police making an arrest, or even a parent carrying a misbehaving child to their room.
    🎮 Analogy: The bigger sibling who can physically move you off the couch when you won't get up.

🌟 Bonus: Charismatic Power

Some people don't need force or money – they have that special "it factor" that makes others want to follow them.

Think of that one student who can convince the whole class to ask for a test postponement. No one elected them, they don't have money – people just listen because of who they are.

Real example: Nelson Mandela inspired millions from a prison cell. That's charisma.

🔑 How Do People Get Power? (The "Origin Stories")

  • Elections (The Democratic Way): People vote. Like student council elections – you convince people, they choose you.
  • Force (The Coup Way): Take power by force. Rare in schools, but happens in some countries when the military says "we're in charge now."
  • Inheritance (The Royal Way): Born into power. Like a king or queen – or like inheriting the family business.
  • Charisma (The Rockstar Way): People follow because you're inspiring. Like a popular musician who starts speaking about politics and people listen.
  • Money (The Billionaire Way): Wealth can buy influence. Think of someone who funds a big project and gets a say in how it runs.
  • Position (The Official Way): The job itself gives power. Being principal gives you power over teachers and students.
Getting power is like leveling up in a game. Some players earn it (elections), some cheat codes (force), some are born with special gear (inheritance), and some just have mad skills everyone respects (charisma).

📱 Power in Everyday Life

  • Group project: The person who decides the topic and assigns tasks – that's political power.
  • Lunch table: The person who decides where everyone sits – that's social power.
  • Family decision: The parent who says "we're not buying that" – economic power.
  • Classroom: The teacher who says "phones away" – positional power.
  • Friend group: The person everyone copies – charismatic power.

🇳🇬 Power in Nigeria

The President holds political power – elected by the people.
Dangote (Africa's richest man) holds economic power – his business decisions affect the whole country.
The Nigerian Army holds military power – they defend against Boko Haram.
The Police hold physical power – they arrest lawbreakers.
Fela Kuti (musician) had charismatic power – his music influenced politics even though he wasn't a politician.

📖 Power in Action – A Quick Story

The School Project: Four students need to present a project. Chioma is the group leader (political power – elected). Emeka's dad owns a printing press, so he offers to print the handouts (economic power). Sarah is super persuasive and gets everyone excited (charismatic power). When Tunde refuses to do his part, Chioma reminds him the teacher will mark him down – that's using positional power. Each type of power works together (or sometimes clashes) to get things done.

To sum up: Power = getting your way. It comes in different flavors – political, economic, military, physical, charismatic. People get it through elections, force, inheritance, charisma, money, or position. Understanding power helps you see who really runs things – in government, school, or even your friend group.
