Topic 1.4

Sovereignty

Jean Bodin (1530–1596)

French philosopher who first developed the modern idea of sovereignty. He argued that in every state there must be a single, ultimate power that makes laws and cannot be overruled.

His key idea: "Sovereignty is the absolute and perpetual power of a state – the power to make law without consent from above."

🎥 Who was Jean Bodin? (Philosophy explained)

A quick explainer on Jean Bodin and his idea of sovereignty. (Big Think)

Have you ever been in an argument where someone says "because I said so" – and that's the end of it? That's like sovereignty. In a country, sovereignty is the ultimate "because I said so" – the final, highest authority that no one can overrule.

Sovereignty (simple definition): The supreme power of a state to make laws and govern itself without interference from outside. It means a country is the boss of itself.

Think of a video game. The game developer makes the rules, decides what's allowed, and no player can change them. That's sovereignty – the developer is the supreme authority. In the real world, the state is the "developer" of its own territory.

📜 Origin: The idea of sovereignty was first fully developed by Jean Bodin in his 1576 book Six Books of the Commonwealth. He wrote it during a time of religious wars in France, arguing that chaos ends only when there's one clear, supreme authority.

🔑 Five Characteristics of Sovereignty (What Makes It Supreme)

  • 1. Absoluteness – Sovereign power is unlimited. There's no higher legal authority. The state can make any law it wants (as long as it has the power to enforce it).
    🏫 School analogy: The Ministry of Education can change the national curriculum for all schools. No school can say "no, we won't follow it."
  • 2. Permanence – Sovereignty lasts as long as the state exists. Governments come and go, but sovereignty remains.
    🏫 School analogy: Different principals come and go, but the school itself (and its authority) continues.
  • 3. Indivisibility – You can't split sovereignty. There can't be two ultimate authorities in the same state.
    🏫 School analogy: A school can't have two principals both giving final orders – chaos!
  • 4. Exclusivity – Only the state has sovereignty. No other group (church, company, foreign power) can claim supreme authority within its territory.
  • 5. Inalienability – The state cannot give away its sovereignty. It can delegate powers, but the ultimate authority stays with the state.

📚 Six Types of Sovereignty

Sovereignty isn't one-size-fits-all – it shows up in different forms:

Legal Sovereignty

The body that has the final legal power to make laws. In Nigeria, the National Assembly has legal sovereignty – it can pass any law (within constitutional limits).

Political/Popular Sovereignty

The people are the ultimate source of power. In a democracy, voters elect leaders – political sovereignty lies with the citizens.

Internal Sovereignty

The state's supreme authority within its own borders – making and enforcing laws.

External Sovereignty

The state's independence from outside control – other countries recognize its right to govern itself.

📍 Where Does Sovereignty Reside? (Location)

It depends on the type of government:

  • In a democracy: The people are sovereign (popular sovereignty).
  • In a monarchy: The monarch is sovereign (historically). Today, many monarchs are ceremonial.
  • In a federal system (like Nigeria): Sovereignty is shared between central and state governments (but the constitution is supreme).
  • In a unitary system (like UK/France): Sovereignty is centralized in the national government.
  • In a military regime: Sovereignty resides with the military leadership (e.g., the Provisional Ruling Council in past Nigerian regimes).
In a school: Is sovereignty with the principal? The school board? The government? Actually, it's shared – the principal has daily authority, but the school board sets policy, and the Ministry sets national standards. That's like federalism – sovereignty is layered.

🚫 Is Sovereignty Really Absolute? (20 Limitations – but we'll make it snappy!)

No state is completely free – there are always limits. Think of it like this: even the strongest person has things they can't do. Here are the "leashes" on sovereignty:

🏠 Internal Limits (from inside the country)

  • 📜 The constitution: Even the president must follow the rulebook.
  • 🗳️ The electorate: Voters can say "you're fired!" at elections.
  • 📢 Public opinion: Governments that ignore protests don't last.
  • ⚔️ The military: Can stage a coup if they disagree (unfortunately).
  • 👴 Customs & traditions: Some things are just "how we do things" – government can't change them overnight.
  • ⛪ Religion & culture: Governments often respect religious laws (e.g., Sharia in some Nigerian states).
  • 📦 Delegated legislation: When parliament gives power to local councils, it limits itself.
  • 💰 Resources: A poor country can't build a space programme just because it wants to.
  • 📣 Pressure groups: NLC (unions) can shut down the economy with strikes.
  • 🏛️ Type of government: Federal systems share power – no one is absolute.

🌍 External Limits (from outside)

  • 🇺🇳 International organizations: UN, AU, ECOWAS – members must follow the rules.
  • ⚖️ International law: Treaties on human rights, environment – can't just ignore them.
  • 🤝 Global interdependence: No country is an island – we need trade and friends.
  • 🦅 Powerful states: Big brothers (USA, China) can pressure smaller ones.
  • 🛡️ Military alliances: NATO, ECOWAS – you commit to collective action.
  • 💵 Foreign aid: Donors often say "if we give you money, you must do X."
  • 🏦 IMF & World Bank: They impose conditions on loans (austerity, reforms).
  • 🎩 Diplomatic immunity: Foreign diplomats can't be arrested – limits state power.
  • 📦 Trade agreements: WTO rules limit tariffs and quotas.
  • 🗺️ Geography: Landlocked countries depend on neighbours for ports.

🇳🇬 Sovereignty in Nigeria – Real Examples

  • Internal limitation: The Nigerian constitution limits what the president can do – can't declare martial law without approval.
  • External limitation: As a member of ECOWAS, Nigeria must consult other West African states on regional security (like interventions in Gambia, Liberia).
  • IMF/World Bank: When Nigeria takes loans, it sometimes has to follow economic reform programs – a limit on economic sovereignty.
  • Military coups: Between 1966 and 1999, the military repeatedly overrode civilian sovereignty.
  • Traditional rulers: The Sultan of Sokoto has moral authority that limits what government can do in northern Nigeria.

🔴 REAL NEWS: Niger Coup (July 2023) – Sovereignty vs. Regional Pressure

📍 Niger | July 2023

In July 2023, military officers in Niger overthrew the democratically elected president, Mohamed Bazoum. They declared themselves the new rulers – they had de-facto sovereignty (they controlled the ground).

But ECOWAS (led by Nigeria) condemned the coup and threatened military intervention to restore democracy. Niger's junta refused to back down, and countries like France and the US paused aid.

What does this teach us? Sovereignty isn't simple. The junta had internal control (de-facto), but they lacked external recognition (de-jure). Regional and international pressure showed that even a sovereign state can't act completely alone – external limitations are real.

As of 2024, Niger's junta remains in power, but the country faces sanctions and isolation – a clear example of sovereignty under pressure.

📖 Sovereignty in Action – The School Analogy

"The Principal's Dilemma" – Principal Adebayo thought he had absolute power in his school. But then:

  • The school board rejected his budget (constitutional limit).
  • Students protested his new uniform policy (public opinion).
  • The Ministry of Education announced a new national curriculum he had to follow (external limit).
  • Parents threatened to withdraw their children (pressure group).

Principal Adebayo learned what every sovereign power learns: absolute sovereignty is an ideal, not reality. Every power has limits.

To sum up: Sovereignty = supreme power of a state to rule itself. It has five key traits (absolute, permanent, indivisible, exclusive, inalienable). But in reality, every state faces limits – from constitutions, voters, international law, and powerful neighbors. Remember: de‑jure is legal right, de‑facto is actual control. Understanding sovereignty helps us see why countries fight so hard for independence – and why no country is truly 100% free.
