Topic 1.8

Nation-State

🇩🇪
Otto von Bismarck (1815–1898)

German chancellor who helped create the modern nation-state of Germany. Before him, Germany was many small states. He brought them together under one government.

His famous quote: "The great questions of the day will not be decided by speeches and majority decisions – but by blood and iron." He meant that building a nation-state sometimes requires tough action.

Have you ever wished that everyone in your country shared the same culture and spoke the same language? That's the idea behind a nation-state – when the borders of a state match the homeland of a nation.

Definition of a Nation-State: A nation-state is a type of state where the citizens are mostly the same in terms of language, culture, and identity – they form a single nation. The state's borders match the territory where that nation lives.

Think of a nation-state like a school where every student wears the same uniform, shares the same school song, and feels proud to be part of that school. The school (state) and the student body (nation) are perfectly matched.

🇯🇵 Japan – The Classic Nation-State

Japan is often called the perfect example of a nation-state. Why? Because:

  • Language: Almost everyone speaks Japanese.
  • Ethnicity: About 98% of people are ethnic Japanese.
  • Culture: Shared traditions, food, festivals, and values.
  • History: Long history as a unified country with an emperor.

In Japan, the "nation" (Japanese people) and the "state" (the country of Japan) are almost the same thing.

🇺🇸 USA – NOT a Nation-State (But a Different Kind)

The United States is not a nation-state. Why? Because it contains many different nations within its borders – people from all over the world with different languages, cultures, and backgrounds. Instead, the USA is a multi-national state (like Nigeria).

Think of a nation-state like a pure fruit juice – 100% orange, no mixing. The USA is more like a fruit salad – many different fruits (nations) in one bowl (state). Both are good, but they're different!

🇳🇬 Nigeria – A Multi-National State

As we've seen, Nigeria is home to many nations: Hausa, Igbo, Yoruba, and over 250 other ethnic groups. That makes Nigeria a multi-national state – one state containing multiple nations.

⚔️ When Nations Don't Have a State – Stateless Nations

Some nations dream of having their own state, but don't. These are called stateless nations.

Real Examples of Stateless Nations:

  • 🇰🇷🇰🇵 The Kurds: About 30 million Kurds live across Turkey, Iraq, Iran, and Syria. They share a language and culture, but have no country of their own.
  • 🏴󠁧󠁢󠁳󠁣󠁴󠁿 The Scots: Scotland is part of the United Kingdom, but many Scots see themselves as a separate nation. They had a vote on independence in 2014.
  • 🇪🇸 The Catalans: In Spain, Catalonia has its own language and culture. Some Catalans want their own state.
  • 🌍 The Palestinians: Palestinians live in the West Bank and Gaza, but many see themselves as a nation without full statehood.

🔑 Why Does the Nation-State Matter?

The idea of the nation-state is powerful because:

  • Unity: When people share language and culture, it's often easier to agree on how to run the country.
  • Independence: Many nations have fought for their own state (like Poland, which disappeared from maps for over 100 years, then reappeared).
  • Conflict: When nations are forced into states they don't want, it can cause problems (like in Yugoslavia, which broke apart in the 1990s).

📖 The Story of Poland – A Nation That Refused to Die

Poland disappeared from the map in 1795, divided between Russia, Prussia, and Austria. For 123 years, there was no Polish state. But the Polish nation survived – people kept speaking Polish, celebrating their culture, and dreaming of independence. In 1918, after World War I, Poland reappeared as a nation-state. This shows that a nation can exist even without a state – but the nation-state is when they finally get their own home.

📖 A Story: The Perfect Match

"The School That Fit Just Right" – Imagine a school built for one big family. Everyone speaks the same language at home, celebrates the same holidays, and shares the same traditions. The school rules reflect what families already believe. Students feel like the school is theirs.

That's a nation-state – when the country (school) fits the people (families) perfectly.

Now imagine a school where students come from 50 different family traditions. The school has to work harder to make everyone feel included. That's a multi-national state like Nigeria or the USA. Both can work – they just need different approaches.

To sum up: A nation-state is when the borders of a state match the homeland of a nation. Japan is a classic example. But many countries (like Nigeria and the USA) are multi-national states – they contain many nations within one state. And some nations (like the Kurds) are still waiting for their own state. Understanding nation-states helps us see why some countries are peaceful and united, while others struggle with division.
