Topic 1.6

The State

📚
Max Weber (1864–1920)

German sociologist who gave one of the most famous definitions of the state: "a human community that successfully claims the monopoly of the legitimate use of physical force within a given territory."

His key idea: The state is the only institution that can legitimately use force – that's what makes it special.

Definition of the State: A politically organised body of people inhabiting a defined geographical entity with an organised legitimate government. The state is entirely free from external control and has the power to secure obedience from its citizens.

Think of the state like a football club. It has a name (like "Nigeria" or "Manchester United"), a home ground (territory), fans (citizens), a manager (government), and rules (laws). The club continues even when managers change – just like the state continues when governments change.

Important Distinction: A state is different from empires, kingdoms, and chieftains. The modern state is characterized by defined boundaries, sovereign authority, and organized government.

🏛️ Six Essential Features of a State

For something to be a real state (not just a group of people), it must have all six of these:

🏛️
1. Permanence

The state remains permanent even when governments change.

🗺️
2. Defined Territory

Clear boundaries – land, water, air space.

⚖️
3. Government

Machinery that runs the state's affairs.

👑
4. Sovereignty

Supreme power – no external control.

👥
5. Population

People who live within the territory.

🤝
6. Recognition

Accepted by other states and its own people.

  • 🏛️ 1. Permanence The state remains a permanent feature. Governments can change many times but a state remains permanent. This continuity ensures stability and ongoing national identity despite political transitions.
    🏫 School analogy: Different principals come and go, but the school itself continues.
  • 🗺️ 2. Defined Territory A state has a defined territory with a limit to its size. It must have clearly defined boundaries, separating it from others. Waters, mountains, forests, and air space all constitute the state's territory.
    🏫 School analogy: The school has a fence – that's its territory. Everyone knows where the school ends and the neighbourhood begins.
  • ⚖️ 3. Government Though government is not permanent, it remains a significant feature as a machinery set up by the state to pilot the affairs of the people. The sustenance and prestige of a state are maintained by the government.
  • 👑 4. Sovereignty The supreme power to make decisions and enforce them on the people is with the state. The sovereignty of a state is not negotiable and must be free from external interference and control.
  • 👥 5. Population This is an important attribute of a state. For a state to be called a state, there must be a given number of people making up the entity. However, with time, such number is bound to increase.
  • 🤝 6. Recognition A state must be recognised both internally and externally. Internal recognition comes from its citizens, while external recognition comes from other states and international organizations.

02 🔄 Relationship between State and Government

The state and government are inseparable. The state is the big picture; the government is the day-to-day management.

The State

  • ✅ Permanent entity
  • ✅ Corporate body
  • ✅ Holds sovereignty
  • ✅ Comprises people and territory

The Government

  • 🔄 Changeable institution
  • 🔄 Administrative machinery
  • 🔄 Exercises sovereignty in trust
  • 🔄 Has three organs (Executive, Legislative, Judicial)

🔑 Key Point:

No state exists without a government, otherwise anarchy would reign. Government can change as many times as possible but the state remains a permanent feature. The sovereignty of the state is rested with the government, which exercises it in trust for the state.

03 🌍 State and Nation

State

A state exists where a territory, a people, a government, and sovereignty exist. It may lack the feeling of nationality or oneness among the people and yet remain a state.

The state may accommodate people of different historical backgrounds, languages, cultures, and religious beliefs, as well as people of homogeneous identity.

Nation

A nation is a group of people who share a common identity – usually language, culture, history, or ethnicity. They feel a sense of unity and belonging.

Example: The Kurdish people consider themselves a nation, even though they don't have their own state.

🇳🇬 Nigeria as a State

Nigeria is a state – it has territory, population, government, and sovereignty. But within Nigeria, there are many nations: Hausa, Igbo, Yoruba, etc. They share a common Nigerian identity (citizenship) but also maintain their own cultural identities. This is what makes Nigeria a multi-national state.

📖 A School Story

"The School That Never Closes" – Government Secondary School, Abuja, opened in 1980. Since then, five different principals have led the school. Students graduate, teachers retire, but the school keeps going.

This is just like how a state works. The school is like the state – it stays. The principals are like governments – they change. The school has land (territory), students and staff (population), a principal (government), and rules from the Ministry (sovereignty). The school doesn't disappear when a new principal arrives – and neither does a state when a new government takes over.

To sum up: A state is a permanent political entity with defined territory, population, government, and sovereignty. It's different from a government (which is temporary) and a nation (which is about shared identity). States need recognition to truly exist. Understanding the state helps us see why countries matter – and why they don't disappear just because leaders change.
