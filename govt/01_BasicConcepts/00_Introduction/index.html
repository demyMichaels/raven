Understanding Government

Jean Bodin (1530–1596)

French political philosopher who first developed the modern idea of sovereignty – the absolute and permanent power of the state. His work laid the foundation for understanding government as a supreme authority.

Bodin's key idea: "Sovereignty is the absolute and perpetual power of a commonwealth."

Government is the machinery through which the will of the state is formulated, expressed, and realised. It exists because people living together need rules and organisation to prevent chaos and protect the weak from the strong.

Government as an Institution: A machinery established by the state to organise, manage affairs, and administer functions. It is through government that the state exercises its power.

🏛️ Government as an Institution

Government is the body that makes and enforces laws, maintains order, provides public services, and represents the state both internally and externally. It has three organs:

  • Executive: Policy formulation and implementation.
  • Legislature: Law-making.
  • Judiciary: Interpretation of laws.

⚙️ Government as a Process (Art of Governing)

Governing is an art – it involves making decisions, resolving conflicts, and steering society. A government must:

  • Maintain peace and security
  • Make and enforce laws
  • Formulate and implement policies
  • Interpret laws and punish offenders

📚 Government as an Academic Field of Study

Also known as Political Science, it studies political institutions, ideas, values, and behaviour. Branches include:

  • Public Administration: Management of government establishments.
  • International Relations: Diplomacy and external affairs.
  • Political Thought / Theories: Ideas of thinkers like Locke, Hobbes, Machiavelli.
  • Political Economy: Monetary and economic policies.
  • Comparative Politics: Analysis of different government systems.

🔧 Functions of Government

Core Responsibilities

  • Law-making: Ensuring peace and order through legislation.
  • Social amenities: Providing electricity, water, health centres.
  • Maintenance of law and order: Police and security agencies.
  • Defence: Protecting against external aggression.
  • Protection of lives and property: Ensuring safety.
  • Employment: Creating jobs for citizens.
  • Policy formulation & implementation: Steering national development.
  • Economic promotion: Infrastructure and investment encouragement.
  • External affairs: Diplomacy and international relations.
  • Administration of justice: Settling disputes through courts.
  • Unity maintenance: Harmonising ethnic and religious groups.
  • Settlement of disputes: Between individuals, groups, and government.
  • Economic functions: Regulating the economy, boosting industrialisation.
  • Political functions: Conducting free and fair elections.
  • Revenue generation: Taxes, fines, duties to fund services.
  • Fundamental human rights: Protecting rights as per constitution.
  • Infrastructure: Building roads, airports, railways.
  • National interest: Protecting citizens' well‑being in foreign policy.
  • Conduct of elections & census: Through bodies like INEC.

📋 Characteristics of Government

  • Law: Guide to citizens' behaviour, backed by constitution.
  • Political power: Derived from people's will.
  • Revenue: Needed to perform functions.
  • Work-force: Civil service for policy implementation.
  • People's support: Legitimacy brings stability.
  • Welfare services: Provision of essential amenities.
  • Periodic change: Elections ensure no permanent government.
  • Protection: Security of lives and property.

🇳🇬 Nigeria Example

The Nigerian government, through the National Assembly (legislature), makes laws; the executive (President) implements them; the judiciary interprets them. Agencies like INEC conduct elections, and the police maintain order. This shows government as both institution and process.
