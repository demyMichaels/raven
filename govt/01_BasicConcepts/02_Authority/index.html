Topic 1.2

Authority

Max Weber (1864–1920)

German sociologist who figured out that power isn't just about force – it's about whether people ACCEPT it. He identified three types of authority: traditional, charismatic, and legal-rational.

His key idea: "Authority is power that people see as legitimate – not just fear, but belief."

Ever listened to a teacher just because they're the teacher? That's authority. It's power with permission – people follow because they believe they SHOULD, not just because they're scared.

Authority (simple definition): The RIGHT to give orders and have them obeyed. It's like being the referee in a game – players listen because they accept the referee's role, not because the referee is stronger.

Power is having a remote control; authority is everyone agreeing that you're the one who should hold it. A bully might take the remote by force (power), but a parent has the remote because everyone accepts they're in charge (authority).

🔹 Two Main Kinds of Authority

  • Institutional Authority: Comes from the position you hold. Example: The President has authority because of the office, not because of who they are personally. Same with a school principal.
  • Functional Authority: Comes from your role or expertise. Example: A doctor giving medical advice – you listen because they know what they're talking about.

🌊 Where Does Authority Come From? (4 Sources)

  • Traditional Authority – "Because we've always done it this way" Based on customs and traditions. Example: The Oba or Emir in Nigeria – people respect them because of centuries of tradition.
    🏫 School analogy: The tradition that seniors lead the school assembly – it's just always been that way.
  • Legal Authority – "Because the rules say so" Comes from laws and the constitution. Example: The President elected under the Nigerian constitution.
    🏫 School analogy: The class prefect elected by students – they have authority because the school rules say so.
  • Charismatic Authority – "Because that person is amazing" Comes from personal qualities – inspiration, charm, heroism. Example: Nelson Mandela, Martin Luther King Jr.
    🏫 School analogy: That one student everyone looks up to – not because of a title, but because they're just cool, smart, and inspiring.
  • Consent of the People – "Because we choose to follow" People willingly give authority to someone they trust. Example: A community leader respected for helping others.
    🏫 School analogy: A student who organises a study group – everyone listens because they volunteered to lead, and others agreed.

📚 Weber's Three Types of Authority

Max Weber said there are three pure forms – most real leaders mix them.

👑 Traditional Authority

Based on long-standing customs. People obey because "it's always been that way."

  • Examples: Monarchies (kings/queens), tribal chiefs, religious leaders passed down through families.
  • School version: The tradition that the oldest student leads the school choir – no election, just custom.

✨ Charismatic Authority

Based on the leader's extraordinary personal qualities – heroism, vision, speaking ability.

  • Examples: Jesus, Prophet Muhammad, Nelson Mandela, Fela Kuti.
  • School version: The student who starts a movement to reduce plastic waste – people follow because they believe in them.

⚖️ Legal-Rational Authority

Based on laws, rules, and procedures. People obey because they believe in the system, not the person.

  • Examples: Elected presidents, civil servants, judges.
  • School version: The teacher – they have authority because the Ministry of Education says so, not because they're personally amazing (though some are!).

🔹 More Flavors of Authority

  • Delegated Authority: When someone with authority passes it to you. Example: A teacher asks you to take attendance – you now have authority in that moment.
  • Technical Authority: Because you're an expert. Example: The IT guy who says your computer needs a restart – you listen because they know best.
  • Political Authority: Authority from holding political office (president, governor).
  • Positional Authority: Same as institutional – comes from the job title.
  • Coercive Authority: Authority based on fear – like a dictator. But is it real authority? Weber would say it's just power, not authority, because people don't accept it willingly.

⚖️ De-jure vs De-facto Authority

De-jure Authority

The LEGAL right to command. You have the title, the law says you're in charge.

Example: A president who was elected but has lost popular support – they still have de-jure authority.

De-facto Authority

The ACTUAL obedience you get. People listen, even if you don't have the legal title.

Example: A rebel leader who controls a region – people obey them, though legally they have no right.

In school: A substitute teacher has de-jure authority (the school hired them). But if students only listen to the popular kid who keeps order, that kid has de-facto authority.

🗣️ Authority in Your Daily Life

  • At home: Your parents have authority – you accept they make rules (usually!).
  • At school: Teachers, principals – you listen because of their position.
  • With friends: The one who always plans hangouts? They have charismatic authority.
  • Online: A YouTuber you trust for tech reviews? Technical authority.
  • In your community: The elder everyone respects? Traditional authority.

🇳🇬 Authority in Nigeria

  • Traditional: The Sultan of Sokoto, Ooni of Ife – people respect their traditional authority.
  • Legal: President Bola Tinubu – authority from the constitution.
  • Charismatic: Fela Kuti – his music gave him moral authority, even though he wasn't a politician.
  • Technical: A professor like Wole Soyinka – people listen on issues of culture because of expertise.

📖 Authority in Action – A School Story

"The New Principal" – When Mr. Adebayo became principal, he had legal authority (the government appointed him). But students didn't automatically respect him. He earned charismatic authority by actually listening to student concerns. The older teachers had traditional authority – they'd been there for years. The ICT teacher had technical authority – if she said a computer was broken, everyone believed her. Authority comes in many forms, and the best leaders have a mix.
