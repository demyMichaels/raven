Discuss their organisation. Explain their functions. Identify different types (e.g. elite and mass). 8.2 POLITICAL PARTY Definition: A political party is defined as an organised group of citizens, who act together as a political unit, having distinctive aims and objectives involving political questions in the state and acting together and seeking to obtain control of government. Secondly, it is also defined as an association of like-minded persons who organise themselves into a political group for the purpose of controlling political power and the administrative machinery of a state. Joseph Schumpeter, in his writing, observed that the first and foremost aim of each political party is to prevail over others in order to get into power or to stay in it. Note: It is the goal of attaining political power that distinguishes political parties from other groups in the political system, e.g. pressure group. In Nigeria's First Republic (1963-66), NPC, NCNC and AG were the prominent political parties that contested elections. However, there were a few other minor political parties. 8.3 CHARACTERISTICS OF POLITICAL PARTIES Organisation: Political parties are organised, with procedure for electing party leaders properly defined. The National Executive Committee responsible for preparing or drawing up the policies and programmes of the party equally coordinates the activities at the local level including important decisions affecting the constituency and the local committees. Manifesto: This is the cardinal plan or programme of a political party which the party intends to fulfil to the people if elected into power. Contesting elections: This is the primary motive of a political party, to contest election and be in government. Nomination: In democratic states, it is the political parties that carry out nomination of candidates and these candidates contest elections under the banner of their political parties. Campaign: During the period of campaigns, political parties publicise their manifestos to the electorate and the purpose of this is to win their support and votes. Ideology: Their philosophy, system of thought and ideas are parts of what the political parties tend to propagate to the electorate. For example, a socialist party would like to transform the state into a welfare socialist state. Primary election: It is an election conducted within a political party to choose popular candidates that can represent the interest of the party in any election. Rallies, congresses and conferences: These are some of the features of a political party. Purposeful leadership: Leaders with purposeful and positive qualities are better at organising political parties and giving them a focus. 8.4 FUNCTIONS / IMPORTANCE OF POLITICAL PARTIES Unity: Political parties unite the people on major issues affecting the state. Serving as a link: Political parties always serve as a link between the people and government. Accountability: They enable the government to become accountable to the electorate through constructive criticism of the government in power. Machinery for recruitment: Political parties provide a machinery for recruiting political leaders. Clarification of issues: Political parties try to clarify issues and provide relevant information on candidates and current events, thereby encouraging the electorate to be more interested in governmental activities by keeping the nation politically alive. Provision of organisation: They try to provide an organisation for running the government. Control of the government: Political parties exist to contested actions and control the government. Dramatisation of politics: This is done by keeping the nation politically alive. Sectional or diversified interests: They harmonise sectional and diverse interests. Change of government: With political parties contesting elections, Change of government is made possible with ease. Political education: It provides political education, through lecture, symposia, manifestoes and other social programmes. Social welfare and humanitarian services: It provides social welfare services for the people, e.g. job opportunities, award of scholarships, etc. Opposition: Political parties provide opposition to the ruling party. Sponsorship: They nominate and sponsor candidates for elections. Training: Political parties equally train future political leaders. Provision of programmes: They provide programmes which they hope to implement when voted into power. They organise rallies, campaigns, lectures, debates and symposia on government programmes and activities. Government also gives funds to them, resulting in waste of public funds. Division of the country: A country with a two-party or multi-party system can divide the country into different opposition camps, and this does not make for unity of a country. Candidates for elections: Nomination of candidates, by political parties for elections, may not have the people's consent or legitimate support. Evil practices: Evil practices of different dimensions could result from political parties, for example, tribalism, sectionalism, victimisation and nepotism. People could be instigated to commit evil, because of being involved in a political party. Appointments: Most appointments made are based on party leaning. Ministers, judges, ambassadors, etc. are appointed according to their loyalty to a particular party in power. Issue of neglect: Neglect in the area of provision of amenities may happen to communities that did not give their votes to the party in government. Apathy: Some people are apathetic to politics. They see political parties as not living up to their expectations, especially in the areas of fulfilling their electoral promises. Party disloyalty: Carpet-crossing is a common feature of political parties. It makes nonsense of the earlier avowed loyalty an individual has made to his party. 8.5 TYPES OR STRUCTURE OF POLITICAL PARTIES Branch/mass parties: This type of party cuts across tribes, interests and individuals. People wishing to become members are free to join. Membership of mass parties are mainly composed of different sections of the society. The programmes or manifestos of such parties are directed towards the welfare of the people. Caucus/elitist parties: It is made up of people of the upper class in the society. These people believe that the control of government should be their preserve. In addition, it should be in the hands of those with distinction and of high intellectual standing. Religious parties: Religious doctrines existing in a country determine the formation of these parties. In some countries, religious parties are very strong in the political process, e.g. Iran. Broker parties: It is a party of both the rich and the poor. They work towards bridging the gap between the rich and the poor, and promoting equality and welfare of the people. Charismatic parties: Individuals with unique and enviable qualities, talent, etc. can form these parties. People are attracted to these parties because of the charisma of those individuals that formed them. For example, NPC of Alhaji Ahmadu Bello, the Sardauna of Sokoto in the First Republic, NCNC of Dr Nnamdi Azikiwe, and A.G. of Chief Obaferni Awolowo. The Organisational Structure of a Political Party Ward smallest unit of the party structure: The officials are Chairman, Secretary and Treasurer. Branches: There are local government branches throughout the country if the party is a very strong party with mass followership. Constituency branches: It includes a Chairman, Organising Secretary, Treasurer, etc. Affiliation: There are other affiliated bodies. Regional/State branches: They coordinate the activities of all constituency branches. National conference: This conference is responsible for national delegates conference. National Working Committee: This committee makes decisions or deals with a particular subject. National Secretariat: It is responsible for running the affairs of the party. National Executive Committee: It is responsible for preparing or drawing up the policies and programmes of the party and equally coordinates the activities at the local level. Board of Trustees: This board controls the financial affairs of the party. General Convention/Congress: A large formal meeting or series of meetings where representatives from different groups make decisions. It is also the gathering of members to elect candidates for different elections, e.g. presidential and governorship. Factors which Determine the Electoral Success of a Political Party Manifesto: If a political party has a good manifesto, this may produce overwhelming support for the party at elections. Realistic past performance: A good track record of a political party may give it an edge over others. Spread of membership: A political party has more chances of victory at the polls if followership cuts across the society. Organisation: A well planned organisational structure can produce better chances for a party at elections. Leadership quality: If the leaders of a political party are capable and have vision, the party can make a headway at the polls. Quality of candidates: The type of candidates presented by a party to contest elections may make or mar the party's chances at the polls. Adequate funding: A party with enough fund can organise the best of campaigns and rallies by engaging the services of an advertising agency for a well produced party emblem and jingles, etc. These may help in recording successes at the polls. Party patronage: If a political party can win the support of some organisations, wealthy or influential individuals in the society, its chances at the polls may be enhanced. Unity: This can help to brighten the chances of the party at elections. Sensitisation: Massive sensitisation and education of the electorate by the party. Party discipline: Very strong party discipline determines the electoral success of a political party. Auxiliary bodies: These are well organised auxiliary bodies within the party such as the youth and women's wings. Effective propaganda: Machinery of a political party determines the electoral success, e.g. Press. Transparent electoral process: This is also a factor that can determine the electoral success of a political party. 8.6 PROBLEMS OF POLITICAL PARTIES Anti-party activities: Some party members are disloyal and involved in anti-party activities. Irresponsible acts: Most of the party leaders are irresponsible. Many of them were imposed on party members or faithful. Corruption and favouritism: Political parties are always guilty of nepotism, bribery and corruption. Factionalisation: Factionalisation within any political party has the capacity of weakening its cohesion. Inadequate funding: Poor and inadequate funding has over time affected the smooth running of mostly the small parties and their successes at the polls. Imposition: Imposition of ideas and candidates on political parties by influential members. Poor organisation: The bane of most political parties is poor party organisation or management. Educational Material on Political Parties PARTY SYSTEMS Educational Material 9.1 PERFORMANCE OBJECTIVES At the end of this chapter, students should be able to: Explain party system. Differentiate political parties and party system. Discuss the characteristics of different systems. Explain the merits and demerits of party system. 9.2 PARTY SYSTEM Definition: Party system may be defined as the conduct of government based on the number of political parties within the system, for example, NPN, UPN, NPP, GNPP, PRP and NAP, the six political parties of the Second Republic (1979-83). The six political parties are an example of multi-party system. There are three types of party systems: one-party, two-party and multi-party systems. 9.3 ONE-PARTY OR SINGLE PARTY SYSTEM Definition: Single party may be defined as one in which only one legal and legitimate political party is constitutionally recognised in the country. No other party is recognised and can therefore not exist. A one-party system is a common feature of communist or socialist countries, for example, North Korea, Cuba and China. In Africa, Malawi, Kenya, Zambia, etc. were formerly one-party states. Types of One-party System True single-party system: The law effectively prevents the formation of any other active parties. By definition, this party system does not accept or permit opposition. This type of system was operative in Malawi, Zambia, etc. Hegemonic party: One party is firmly established in office. The party has hegemony, that is, it enjoys unchallenged authority. The law does not operate formally against any opposition, but clearly the state concerned is effectively single party for period of many years. For example, South Africa was an example of a hegemonic party (all - white electorate). The National Party came into power in 1948, and it only left power when Dr Nelson Mandela, was elected president in 1994. Predominant party: This is a party which is elected and continues to be elected because the electorate so desire. This type of party is mostly found in Asian countries. Characteristics of a One-party State One officially recognised party: There is no other officially recognised political party, apart from the legally recognised party that can contest elections. Recognised by the constitution: Only this legal party is recognised by the constitution. No private control of the mass media: No private individual is allowed to own and publish newspaper, magazine, etc. One ideology: There is only one ideology for the country and it is for the party in government. Enforcement of government policies: The party in government ensures that all her policies and directives are strictly enforced. Opposition party: Opposition party is not legally recognised. Dictatorship: A one-party state can develop to become dictatorship. Merits of One-party System Prevention of economic waste It makes use of the best human resources Promotion of unity Stability of government Makes for quick decisions Continuity in government Minimised electoral malpractice Absence of unhealthy rivalry It makes for strong leadership Demerits of One-party System No opposition Individual rights are not accommodated Control of mass media It may produce dictatorship It is basically undemocratic It can produce "a president for life" Only one ideology Rule of law may not be respected Change of government not provided for Difference between De jure and De facto One-party System De jure means only one party is legally and official recognised in the state while in de facto, other parties may exist, but only one party dominates the political scene. 9.5 TWO-PARTY SYSTEM Definition: A two-party system may be defined as the existence of only two major political parties in a political system. These two parties are constitutionally recognised to contest elections. Britain is a good example of a two-party state. There are two major political parties – Labour and Conservative parties. Liberal party is a minor party. USA, Canada, Australia, etc. also practise a two-party system. Features of Two-party System Two major political parties: There are only two major political parties that are legally and constitutionally recognised. However, there may be in existence more than two political parties contesting elections. Opposition party: The opposition party is legally recognised in a two-party system. Choice: There is the opportunity for choice of candidates and parties. It is democratic: It accommodates democratic principles and allows the operation of the rule of law. Press freedom: A two-party system allows for press freedom, i.e. no censorship. Change of government: Change of government is possible because there is the provision for periodic elections. Corrective party: The opposition party is seen as a corrective party in government. Merits of Two-party System Choice between political parties Provision of strong opposition It makes for good governance It ensures stability It is democratic Easy identification of strengths and weaknesses Change of government is possible Difficult to degenerate into dictatorship Promotes popular government Demerits of Two-party System Division of the country into two factions It does not promote unity It may lead to a one-party state Electoral malpractices Unhealthy party rivalry Waste of the nation's resources Can breed irresponsible opposition May hinder development of minor parties Party loyalty above national interest May lead to victimisation 9.7 MULTI-PARTY SYSTEM Definition: It is defined as a system with more than two political parties are contesting for political power in a country. All the parties are duly registered and recognised by law. For example, Nigeria's Second Republic (1979-83) had six recognised political parties: NPN, UPN, NPP, GNPP, PRP and NAP. In a multi-party system, a coalition government may be formed, if no party had absolute majority seats in parliamentary election. However, coalition government is mostly common with parliamentary democracy. Nigeria, France, Italy, Zambia, Malawi, etc. practise multi-party systems. Features of Multi-party System Existence of many parties: There are more than two political parties in the system. Legal recognition: All the political parties are recognised by the law. Choice: Individuals can easily make choice of parties and programmes. Political education: The scope of political education is enhanced and widened. Wider representation: People of different interests and opinions are represented. Coalition government: May give rise to a coalition government. Absence of dictatorship: There is no place for dictatorship. Periodic election: Elections are conducted at periodic intervals. Protection of human rights: The rights of individuals are guaranteed. Respect for rule of law: There is respect for the rule of law. Merits of Multi-party System It is democratic Wider political participation and education Wider representation Reduction of single party dictatorship Encourages better party organisation Recognition of opposition parties Change of government is possible Human rights are protected Respect for rule of law Wide range of choices for electorate Ensures political accountability Addresses minority interest Encourages healthy competition Caters for all political opinions Reflects political differences Demerits of Multi-party System Often leads to weak coalition governments Too many political programmes can confuse people Difficult to hold a particular party responsible May produce ethnic and sectional parties Makes formation of new government difficult Very expensive party system Victimisation of political opponents Political instability Weakens the executive Can lower standard of public life Roles of Minority Parties in a Multi-party Democracy They provide avenues for alternative government They project the interest of minority groups They help to promote unity and tolerance They keep the government accountable They help to prevent violation of Human Rights They offer useful suggestions for policies They contribute to parliamentary debates They serve on various parliamentary committees They assist in national consensus building They mobilise against unpopular policies They exhibit loyalty and patriotism in crises They protect the constitution and rule of law They provide alternative policies and programmes 9.8 ZERO-PARTY SYSTEM Meaning: A zero-party system has to do with a standard where political leaders are elected by the people on an individual basis. There is no political party. Candidates are mostly independent and are elected according to their merits. Merits of Zero-party System Proper management of time Reduced electoral malpractices Simple to operate Reduced cost of elections Easy choice for electorate Quick and purposeful decision-making Conservation of resources Demerits of Zero-party System Irresponsible political leaders Poorly organised elections Absence of party manifesto Political instability Absence of political education 9.9 PARTY MANIFESTO Definition: A party manifesto may be defined as the cardinal plans or programmes of a political party which the party intends to fulfil to the electorate if it is elected into power. It can also be referred to as the aims and objectives of a political party. Importance or Merit of a Party Manifesto What to do and how to do it: It gives direction to the party's programmes of action and or ideology and stipulates how to achieve them. Manifesto acts as magnetic pull: Party manifesto is usually a magnetic pull to the electorate, i.e. attract more members or supporters to the party. It educates the electorate: Manifesto educates the electorate to have an insight into the aims and objectives and planned programmes of the political party. Assessment: Party manifesto helps the electorate to assess the party on the basis of the contents of the manifesto.
