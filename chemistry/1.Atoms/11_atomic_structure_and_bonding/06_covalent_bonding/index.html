2.6 Covalent Bonding

Sharing is caring (molecular orbitals & hybridization)
🤝
Unlike ionic bonding (where atoms steal electrons), covalent bonding is about sharing electrons to get that stable octet.

2.6.1 Molecular Orbitals: σ and π Bonds

The simplest example? Two hydrogen atoms coming together to form H₂. When atomic orbitals overlap, electrons get shared between both atoms – that's a molecular orbital.

🧪 Bonding MO: lower energy, electrons between atoms → stability.
⚠️ Antibonding MO*: higher energy, electrons excluded from between atoms → no bond.

[Figure 2.5: Two H atoms combining to form H₂ – orbital overlap]
Overlap of 1s orbitals creates a bonding molecular orbital.

Think of it like solving x² = 1: you get two answers (+1 and -1). Similarly, combining atomic orbitals gives two molecular orbitals – one bonding (additive) and one antibonding (subtractive).

[Figure 2.6: Bonding (ø₁+ø₂) vs. antibonding (ø₁-ø₂) orbital]
Same phase → bonding; opposite phase → antibonding.

σ (sigma) Bonds

When s orbitals overlap end‑to‑end, we get a σ bond – it's cylindrically symmetrical around the bond axis. Every single bond is a σ bond.

π (pi) Bonds

When p orbitals overlap sideways, we get a π bond. Electron density is above and below the bond axis, with a nodal plane through the nuclei.

[Figure 2.7: Side‑to‑side overlap of p orbitals → π bond]
Same‑phase lobes overlap → π bond; opposite phases → π* antibonding.

Double bonds = one σ + one π. Triple bonds = one σ + two π. π bonds are more reactive – they're the business end of alkenes and alkynes.

2.6.2 Hybrid Orbitals in Carbon

🧠 A word of caution: Students often find hybridization tricky. But remember – it's just a model we use to explain experimental facts. If the simple atomic orbital picture doesn't fit, we refine it.

The Problem with Plain Carbon

Carbon's ground state is 1s²2s²2px¹2py¹ – only two unpaired electrons. That would suggest carbon forms only two bonds (divalent). But we know carbon is tetravalent (forms four bonds). Something's missing.

Plus, experiments show:

  • Methane (CH₄) is tetrahedral (109.5°), all bonds identical.
  • Ethylene (C₂H₄) is planar (120°), has a π bond.
  • Acetylene (C₂H₂) is linear (180°), has two π bonds.

To explain this, we need hybrid orbitals – a mix of s and p orbitals that give the right shape and bonding.

sp³ Hybridization (Methane, Ethane)

Mix one 2s + three 2p → four equivalent sp³ orbitals. They point to the corners of a tetrahedron (109.5° apart).

[Figure 2.8: Promotion of 2s electron to 2p → excited state carbon]
[Figure 2.9: Mixing one s + three p → four sp³ hybrids]
[Figure 2.10: Tetrahedral shape of sp³ orbitals]

Each sp³ orbital gets one electron. Overlap with H 1s orbitals gives four C–H σ bonds – all identical, pointing tetrahedrally.

[Figure 2.11: Bonding in methane – four σ bonds]
[Figure 2.12: Bonding in ethane – C–C σ bond from sp³–sp³ overlap]
CH₄ (methane)
tetrahedral, 109.5°
C₂H₆ (ethane)
free rotation around C–C

sp² Hybridization (Ethylene)

Mix one 2s + two 2p → three equivalent sp² orbitals in a plane (120° apart). The remaining p orbital is perpendicular to that plane.

[Figure 2.13: Electronic configuration for sp² carbon]
[Figure 2.14: Three sp² hybrids (trigonal planar) + one pure p orbital]

In ethylene, each carbon uses sp² orbitals to form σ bonds (one C–C, two C–H). The remaining p orbitals overlap sideways to form a π bond.

[Figure 2.15: Bonding in ethylene – σ framework + π bond]

🔬 Key: π bonds require parallel p orbitals. Twisting breaks the π bond – that's why alkenes have restricted rotation and show cis‑trans isomerism.

[Figure 2.16: Parallel p orbitals overlap (bonding); perpendicular p orbitals don't]

sp Hybridization (Acetylene)

Mix one 2s + one 2p → two equivalent sp orbitals in a line (180° apart). Two pure p orbitals remain, perpendicular to each other and to the sp axis.

[Figure 2.17: Electronic configuration for sp carbon]
[Figure 2.18: Two sp orbitals (linear) + two perpendicular p orbitals]

In acetylene, each carbon uses sp orbitals for σ bonds (one C–C, one C–H). The two sets of p orbitals form two π bonds at right angles.

[Figure 2.19: Bonding in acetylene – σ framework + two π bonds]

2.6.3 Hybrid Orbitals in Oxygen and Nitrogen

The same hybridization concepts apply to N and O.

[Figure 2.20: Electronic configurations for sp³ nitrogen and oxygen]
NH₃
Ammonia
sp³, trigonal pyramidal, one lone pair
H₂O
Water
sp³, bent, two lone pairs
[Figure 2.21: Orbital pictures of NH₃ and H₂O showing lone pairs]

Lone pairs take up more space than bonding pairs, so bond angles are slightly less than perfect tetrahedral (NH₃ ~107°, H₂O ~104.5°).

π Bonds with N and O

In C=O (carbonyl) or C=N (imine), the O or N is sp² hybridized, with a π bond formed from p orbital overlap – just like in alkenes.

[Figure 2.22: sp² hybridization in carbonyl oxygen and imine nitrogen]

In cyanides (C≡N), nitrogen is sp hybridized – linear, with a lone pair in an sp orbital and two π bonds.

[Figure 2.23: sp hybridization in nitrile nitrogen]

📌 Non-Polar Covalent Bond: A covalent bond where electrons are shared equally (e.g., H–H, C–H). No charge separation.
