2.2 Bonding & Valency

How atoms link up (the short version)
🧂
Wait, what? Two toxic things (sodium metal + chlorine gas) make the salt on your fries. Chemistry is wild.

Ionic Bonding (aka "Electron Tug-of-War")

Atoms can straight-up steal electrons from each other. When that happens, they become ions (charged atoms) and stick together like magnets.

Na⁺ Cl⁻   (table salt)
Mg²⁺ 2Cl⁻   (magnesium chloride)

⚡ Heads up: Ripping electrons away gets HARDER each time. Carbon? It refuses to do this ionic thing — it prefers sharing (next section).

Covalent Bonding = Sharing is Caring

Instead of stealing, atoms can share electrons. One shared pair = single bond (drawn as a line).

How many bonds can an atom make? That's its VALENCY (fancy word for "bonding capacity").

H 1
O 2
N 3
C 4

Hydrogen makes 1 bond, Oxygen makes 2, Nitrogen makes 3, Carbon makes 4

Single Bonds (Basic Level)

Methane

H | H—C—H | H
4 single bonds

Methanol

H | H—C—O—H | H
All single

Methylamine

H | H—C—N—H | | H H
Single bonds

Double Bonds (Level Up)

Carbon can get fancy and share TWO pairs of electrons with another atom. That's a double bond (two lines).

Ethylene (Ethene)

H H \ / C=C / \ H H
Double bond

Formaldehyde

O || H—C—H
C=O double

Triple Bonds (Pro Mode)

Sometimes carbon goes all-in and shares THREE pairs — a triple bond (three lines).

Acetylene (Ethyne)

H—C≡C—H
Triple bond

Acetonitrile

H | H—C—C≡N | H
C≡N triple

Lewis Dots & Lone Pairs

Old-school chemists used to draw all the electrons as dots. We don't do that anymore (thank goodness), but we still show lone pairs — those are electrons just hanging out not bonding.

🎯 Why lone pairs matter: They help explain how molecules react. Think of them as "spare electrons ready to cause trouble."

The Octet Rule (8 is Great)

✨ Atoms want 8 electrons in their outer shell (like noble gases). Hydrogen is happy with just 2. ✨

That's why they bond — to get that perfect 8. Lose electrons, gain electrons, or share — whatever it takes to get that stable setup.
