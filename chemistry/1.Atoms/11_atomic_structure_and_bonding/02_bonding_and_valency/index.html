2.3 Atomic Orbitals

Where electrons actually hang out
🐄
Think of it like this: A farmer knows his cow is in the field, but not exactly where at any moment. That's kind of like electrons in orbitals – we know the region, not the exact spot.

Atomic orbitals describe the probability of finding an electron in a given space around the nucleus. We can't pin-point an electron exactly – it's not a nice solid particle. Its behavior is wave-like, and we need math (the Schrödinger equation) to understand it.

🧮 The square of the wave function, ø², is proportional to electron density – it tells us where an electron is likely to be.

Quantum Numbers (Electron's ID Card)

Each wave function (orbital) is defined by a set of quantum numbers. Think of them as an electron's address:

  • n (principal) – distance from nucleus, energy level, row in periodic table (1, 2, 3…)
  • l (angular momentum) – shape of orbital (0 = s, 1 = p, 2 = d, 3 = f)
  • ml (magnetic) – orientation in space (-l to +l)
  • s (spin) – electron spin (±½)

s Orbitals (Spherical)

The lowest energy orbital is 1s – a spherical region around the nucleus. The 2s orbital is also spherical, but it's larger and has a node – a region where the probability of finding an electron is zero.

p Orbitals (Dumbbell-shaped)

For n=2 and above, we also get three p orbitals – 2px, 2py, and 2pz. They're shaped like dumbbells aligned along the axes. They have a node at the nucleus and two lobes with opposite phases.

[Figure 2.2: 2px, 2py, 2pz orbitals – dumbbell shapes along axes]
The three degenerate p orbitals (equal energy)

🌀 Degenerate = orbitals with the same energy level (like the three 2p orbitals).

3s, 3p, 3d and Beyond

For atoms like sulfur and phosphorus, we need 3s and 3p orbitals. After that come the more complex 3d orbitals. As n increases, orbitals get larger and electrons are further from the nucleus.

🔬 Why this matters: Understanding 1s, 2s, and 2p orbitals lets us describe bonding for most atoms in organic molecules. The shapes and energies of orbitals explain how atoms connect and why molecules have specific geometries.
