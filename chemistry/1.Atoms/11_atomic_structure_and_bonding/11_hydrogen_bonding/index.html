2.11 Hydrogen Bonding

The weak attraction that runs the world
🌊
Without hydrogen bonds, life wouldn't exist. They keep water liquid, DNA twisted, and proteins folded. They're weak – but together, they're mighty.

What is a Hydrogen Bond?

A hydrogen bond is a weak attraction between:

  • A hydrogen atom that's bonded to an electronegative atom (like O or N) – this H becomes slightly positive (δ⁺)
  • AND a lone pair on another electronegative atom (like O or N) – this atom is slightly negative (δ⁻)

🧲 Think of it as: A "magnetically curious" hydrogen getting attracted to a neighbor with extra electrons.

Example: Water (H₂O)

H₂O
O is δ⁻, H is δ⁺
⋯
H₂O
Another water molecule

The δ⁺ hydrogen of one water is attracted to the δ⁻ oxygen (lone pair) of another. That dotted line (⋯) is the hydrogen bond.

[Figure: Hydrogen bonding between two water molecules – dotted line shows H-bond]
H-bond length is about twice as long as a covalent O–H bond, and MUCH weaker.

How Strong Are Hydrogen Bonds?

  • 💪 Covalent bond (O–H): strong (~460 kJ/mol)
  • 💧 Hydrogen bond (O⋯H): weak (~5-30 kJ/mol)
  • But they add up! Millions of them give water its special properties.

Why Water is Special (Thanks to H‑bonds)

🔥 High boiling point: Without H‑bonds, water would boil at -80°C! Instead, it boils at 100°C because you need extra energy to break all those H‑bonds.

🧊 Ice floats: In ice, H‑bonds form a crystal lattice that's less dense than liquid water. That's why ice floats on your drink.

💊 Great solvent: Water dissolves salts and polar molecules because it can H‑bond to them.

Two Types of Hydrogen Bonds

1. Intermolecular (between molecules)

Like between two water molecules, or in alcohols (RO–H⋯OR). This is what gives liquids their boiling points.

2. Intramolecular (within the same molecule)

Sometimes a molecule can bend so an O–H H‑bonds to another part of itself. Example: the "enol" form of acetylacetone.

[Figure: Intramolecular H-bond in acetylacetone – O–H⋯O inside the same molecule]
The H is stuck between two oxygens – one covalent bond, one H‑bond.

Carboxylic Acids: H‑bonding Pairs

Carboxylic acids (R–COOH) often form dimers (pairs) in solution. Two molecules link up through two H‑bonds – it's like a molecular handshake.

R–C=O⋯H–O
↕️
O–H⋯O=C–R
[Figure: Carboxylic acid dimer – two molecules held together by two H‑bonds]
This dimer is stable enough to exist even in the gas phase!

Why Biologists LOVE Hydrogen Bonds

🧬
DNA: The two strands are held together by H‑bonds between base pairs (A–T and G–C). Just strong enough to keep it together, but weak enough to unzip for replication.
🧪
Proteins: H‑bonds hold α‑helices and β‑sheets in shape. If they break, proteins unravel (denature) – like cooking an egg white.

📌 Remember: Hydrogen bonding requires:

  • H bonded to F, O, or N (very electronegative)
  • A lone pair on another F, O, or N

It's weak alone, but life as we know it depends on it!
