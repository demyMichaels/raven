2.7 Bond Polarity

When sharing isn't equal (the tug-of-war)
🧲
Not all sharing is equal: Some atoms are electron hogs. They pull harder on the shared electrons, creating a polar bond – like a tiny magnet.

Electronegativity: The Electron Tug-of-War

Electronegativity is an atom's ability to attract electrons in a bond. The more electronegative atom pulls the shared electrons closer, creating a charge imbalance – that's bond polarity.

📏 Pauling scale: From Li = 1.0 to F = 4.0. The bigger the number, the stronger the pull.

Showing Bond Polarity

Take a C–Br bond. Bromine is more electronegative than carbon, so it pulls electrons toward itself:

We can show this with:

  • Partial charges (δ⁺ and δ⁻) – "delta" means a little bit of charge.
  • Arrow on the bond – points toward the more electronegative atom.
  • Dipole arrow – crossed end is positive, arrowhead points to negative.

Not Just Ionic or Covalent – A Spectrum

We used to think of bonds as either ionic (electron transfer) or covalent (equal sharing). But most bonds are somewhere in between – polar covalent.

Nonpolar covalent ←───────── | ─────────→ Ionic

(equal sharing)      (polar covalent)      (electron transfer)

Molecular Dipole: The Big Picture

A bond can be polar, but the molecule as a whole might not be – if the bond dipoles cancel out.

CHCl₃
μ = 1.02 D
Has a dipole (polar)
CCl₄
μ = 0 D
Dipoles cancel (nonpolar)

In CCl₄, the four polar C–Cl bonds are arranged tetrahedrally. Their pull cancels out – the molecule has no net dipole.

Inductive Effect: Passing the Polarity Along

A polar bond can influence nearby bonds. In ethyl chloride:

CH₃ → CH₂ → Cl
  • The C–Cl bond is polar (Cl δ⁻, C δ⁺).
  • That δ⁺ on carbon pulls electrons from the C–C bond.
  • The next carbon also becomes slightly positive (δδ⁺ – even smaller).

This transmission of polarity through σ bonds is called the inductive effect. It's short-range – dies out after about 3 carbons – but stronger if you have multiple polar groups (like in CHCl₃, CCl₃CH₃).

[Figure: Inductive effect in ethyl chloride, 1,1-dichloroethane, and 1,1,1-trichloroethane]
More chlorine atoms = stronger inductive effect.

💡 Why it matters: Bond polarity and inductive effects help us predict how molecules will react – where electrons are rich (nucleophilic) and where they're poor (electrophilic). That's the basis of reaction mechanisms.
