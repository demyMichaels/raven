2.8 Conjugation

When double bonds start talking to each other
🧩
Isolated vs. conjugated: Double bonds that are separated by a single bond don't just sit there – they interact. That changes everything – reactivity, color, you name it.

What Does "Conjugated" Mean?

A system is conjugated when double bonds (C=C, C=O, C=N) are separated by exactly one single bond. The p orbitals can then overlap across the whole system.

1,3-Pentadiene
C=C–C=C
Conjugated (alternating double-single-double)
1,4-Pentadiene
C=C–C–C=C
Isolated (double bonds separated by > one single bond)
[Figure 2.24: 1,4-pentadiene – isolated double bonds, no interaction between π systems]
Isolated double bonds: separate π bonds, no overlap.

Why Conjugation Changes Things

In 1,3-pentadiene (and the simpler 1,3-butadiene), the p orbitals on adjacent carbons can all overlap. This creates a delocalized π system – electrons aren't stuck between just two carbons; they spread out over the whole chain.

Molecular Orbitals of 1,3-Butadiene

[Figure 2.25: Four π molecular orbitals of butadiene (ψ₁ to ψ₄) showing bonding/antibonding interactions]
ψ₁: 3 bonding interactions (lowest energy)
ψ₂: 2 bonding, 1 antibonding
ψ₃: 1 bonding, 2 antibonding
ψ₄: 3 antibonding (highest energy)

📊 The four π electrons fill ψ₁ and ψ₂. The total energy is lower than expected – that's the stabilization energy from conjugation.

What Conjugation Does

1️⃣ Delocalization: π electrons are spread over multiple atoms, not stuck between two.

2️⃣ Stability: Conjugated dienes are more stable (lower energy) than isolated dienes.

3️⃣ Different reactivity: They react differently (see Section 8.2 – electrophilic addition vs. Diels–Alder).

4️⃣ Color: As conjugation increases, the energy gap between HOMO and LUMO shrinks – absorption moves from UV into visible light, so compounds appear colored.

Conjugation Isn't Just for Alkenes

Any system with adjacent p orbitals can be conjugated:

  • C=C–C=O (enones – carbonyl conjugated with alkene)
  • C=C–C≡N (unsaturated nitriles)
  • C=C–C⁺ (allylic carbocations – vacant p orbital adjacent to π bond)
  • C=C–C• (allylic radicals – single electron in p orbital)
  • C=C–C⁻ (allylic anions – negative charge in p orbital, even though we might expect sp³)

In carbanions, the negative charge is actually delocalized if there's a π bond next door – the carbon rehybridizes to sp² so the lone pair is in a p orbital and can overlap.

Why Conjugation = Color

As conjugation gets longer (more double bonds in a row), the energy gap between filled and empty π orbitals gets smaller. Electrons need less energy to jump – so they absorb longer wavelengths.

UV ← ... shorter λ, more energy | longer λ, less energy ... → IR

Short conjugated systems (like butadiene) absorb in the UV – they look colorless. Long conjugated systems (like β-carotene, with 11 double bonds) absorb visible light – that's why carrots are orange!

🥕
Real-world example: β-carotene's long conjugated chain absorbs blue/green light, so we see it as orange/red. Tomatoes (lycopene) have even longer conjugation → red.
