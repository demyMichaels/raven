2.1 Atomic Structure

What atoms are made of (the simple version)
🔬
Real talk: You're made of atoms. Your phone is made of atoms. Everything is atoms. Let's see what's inside them.

The Basics

Like we saw in the intro module (1.0 The Mystery of Table Salt), atoms have three main parts:

The classic atom model: protons (+), neutrons (neutral), electrons (-)
  • Protons = positively charged (in the nucleus)
  • Neutrons = neutral, no charge (also in the nucleus)
  • Electrons = negatively charged (zoom around the outside)

In a neutral atom, the number of electrons equals the number of protons. That number is the atomic number (your atom's ID card).

What About Ions?

Sometimes the number of electrons and protons isn't equal. When that happens, the atom gets a charge — that's an ion.

Ions form when electrons are gained or lost

Two types of ions:

  • Anion = negatively charged (gained electrons) — "A Negative Ion" (think A for negative)
  • Cation = positively charged (lost electrons) — "Ca+ion" sounds like "cation"

🧠 Wait — this sounds confusing. Got an analogy?

Imagine you have a kid, but your friend doesn't. On weekends, you send your kid over to keep your friend company. That's like sharing electrons (covalent bonding).

But if your friend fully adopts the kid and takes permanent custody — that's electron transfer (ionic bonding).

In this story: You're the cation (positive, lost the kid), Your friend is the anion (negative, gained the kid).

Ionic bonding = electron transfer. Covalent bonding = electron sharing.

Real Chemical Ions

Na⁺
Sodium ion
Ca²⁺
Calcium ion

Sodium (Na): Atomic number = 11 → 11 protons, 11 electrons (neutral). Lose 1 electron → becomes Na⁺ with 10 electrons and +1 charge.

Calcium (Ca): Atomic number = 20 → 20 protons, 20 electrons (neutral). Lose 2 electrons → becomes Ca²⁺ with 18 electrons and +2 charge.

The Noble Gases (The Cool Kids)

Some atoms are just chill. They don't react much. Meet the noble gases:

Helium (He), Neon (Ne), Argon (Ar) — the "I'm good, I don't need to bond" crew

Why are they so unreactive? They have filled electron shells:

  • Helium: 2 electrons (full shell)
  • Neon: 10 electrons (2 + 8 = full octet)
  • Argon: 18 electrons (2 + 8 + 8 = full shells)

✨ THE OCTET RULE: Atoms want 8 electrons in their outer shell (like noble gases). Hydrogen is happy with 2. ✨

How Atoms Get Stable

To become stable like noble gases, atoms will:

  • LOSE electrons (become positive cations)
  • GAIN electrons (become negative anions)
  • SHARE electrons (covalent bonding — next module!)

Think of Lithium (1 valence electron) — it's HAPPY to give that electron away to be stable like Helium.

Think of Chlorine (7 valence electrons) — it's DESPERATE to grab one more electron to be stable like Argon.

The valence shell = the outer layer where bonding happens. Valence electrons = the ones doing the work.

Valence shell = the unfilled outer shell involved in bonding.

Valence electrons = the electrons in that shell (the ones that actually do something).

Real-World Applications (Because Chemistry Isn't Just in a Textbook)

🥤

Sports Drinks

Electrolytes = ions! They conduct electricity and keep you hydrated.

🏭

Metal Extraction

Aluminum is made by running electricity through molten ions.

🔋

Batteries

Ions moving = electricity flowing.

💧

Water Testing

Conductivity tests measure minerals (ions) in water.

⚠️ Safety moment: The human body contains ions (that's why you're a conductor). This is why electricity + water = DANGEROUS. Your wet skin has ions that let electricity flow through you.

🧪
Try this: Dissolve salt in water and test conductivity. Pure water? No light. Salt water? Light bulb glows — ions are moving!
