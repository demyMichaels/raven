2.5 Ionic Bonding

Electron tug-of-war (with a clear winner)
⚡
The simplest bond to understand: One atom straight-up steals an electron from another. Then they stick together like magnets.

Ionic bonding involves the complete transfer of an electron from one atom to another. The resulting ions (positive and negative) are held together by strong electrostatic attraction – basically, opposites attract.

🎯 The goal: Each atom wants to achieve the electron configuration of the nearest noble gas. One atom loses electrons (becomes positive), the other gains them (becomes negative).

Case Study: Sodium + Chlorine = Table Salt

Na
1s²2s²2p⁶3s¹
→ Na⁺
loses 1 electron
➡️
Cl
1s²2s²2p⁶3s²3p⁵
→ Cl⁻
gains 1 electron

Sodium has one electron more than neon (1s²2s²2p⁶). Chlorine has one electron less than argon (1s²2s²2p⁶3s²3p⁶). Chlorine is highly electronegative (it really wants that electron). So:

  • Na loses its 3s¹ electron → becomes Na⁺ (now has the same configuration as Ne)
  • Cl gains that electron → becomes Cl⁻ (now has the same configuration as Ar)
  • Na⁺ and Cl⁻ stick together like magnets → NaCl (table salt)

The Proton: H⁺

💧 When hydrogen loses its only electron, what's left? Just a proton. So H⁺ is literally a bare proton. Adding H⁺ to something is called protonation.

H: 1s¹ → lose electron → H⁺ (no electrons, just a proton)

More Than One Electron

Some atoms can lose or gain multiple electrons, though it gets harder each time.

Mg
[Ne]3s²
→ Mg²⁺
+
O
[He]2s²2p⁴
→ O²⁻

Magnesium loses two electrons to become Mg²⁺ (now same as Ne). Oxygen gains two electrons to become O²⁻ (now same as Ne). They form MgO (magnesium oxide).

⚠️ Heads up: The more electrons involved, the harder it is to remove them. That's why you don't see things like C⁴⁺ or C⁴⁻ in normal conditions – carbon prefers to share electrons (covalent bonding).

Not Everything is Ionic

Molecules like methane (CH₄) are NOT formed by ionic bonding. Carbon doesn't lose or gain four electrons – that would take way too much energy. Instead, carbon shares electrons with hydrogen. That's our next topic: covalent bonding.
