2.10 Resonance Structures & Curly Arrows

Drawing molecules that can't make up their minds
🤔
Sometimes one drawing isn't enough. Some molecules have "split personalities" – we need multiple structures to show what's really going on. That's resonance.

Resonance: The "Average" Picture

Remember benzene from the last section? We can draw it with double bonds in two different places:

◯
Structure A
↔
◯
Structure B

But the real benzene isn't A or B – it's something in between. All the bonds are the same length (1.40 Å). We call this a resonance hybrid.

The double-headed arrow (↔) is only used for resonance structures. Never use it for anything else!

Curly Arrows: Following the Electrons

A curly arrow shows where electrons are moving. Think of it as a map for electrons:

⤴️
Tail = where electrons start (lone pair, negative charge, or bond)
⤵️
Head = where electrons go (positive charge, electron-hungry atom)

📌 Curly arrow rules:

  • Represents movement of 2 electrons
  • Starts from electron-rich spot (lone pair, negative charge, or bond)
  • Points to electron-poor spot (positive charge, or atom that can accept electrons)

Quick Check: Formal Charge

Before we go further, let's remember how to figure out if an atom has a charge:

Formal charge = (valence electrons in free atom) – (electrons "owned" in the molecule)

Count half of bonding electrons + all lone pairs as "owned" by the atom.

Three Ways Electrons Move

1. Bond → Lone pair

C=O → C⁺–O⁻
⤴️
π bond breaks, electrons go to oxygen

2. Lone pair → Bond

O⁻–C⁺ → O=C
⤵️
Lone pair makes a new π bond

3. Bond → New bond

C=C–C⁺ → C⁺–C=C
🔄
π bond shifts to become a new π bond

Rules for Drawing Resonance Structures

  • ✅ Atoms never move – only electrons move
  • ✅ Must be valid Lewis structures (no pentavalent carbon!)
  • ✅ Overall charge stays the same
  • ✅ Same number of unpaired electrons

Which Resonance Structure is "Better"?

Not all resonance structures are equal. Some contribute more to the real molecule:

More stable (contributes more)Less stable (contributes less)
More covalent bondsFewer covalent bonds
All atoms have octetsAtoms missing octets
No charge separationSeparated charges
Negative charge on electronegative atom (O, N)Negative charge on less electronegative atom (C)

Real Examples

Carbonyl group (C=O)

C=O
↔
C⁺–O⁻

The second structure (with charges) contributes less, but it explains why the carbon is slightly positive and reacts with electron-rich reagents.

Allylic cation

CH₂=CH–CH₂⁺
↔
⁺CH₂–CH=CH₂

Both structures are identical – they contribute equally. The positive charge is spread (delocalized) over both ends.

⚠️ Don't be fooled! Resonance structures are NOT flipping back and forth. The real molecule is a hybrid – like a mule is a mix of horse and donkey, but not switching between them.
