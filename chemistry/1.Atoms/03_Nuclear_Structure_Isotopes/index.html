⚛️ What's Inside the Nucleus?

In Topic 1.2, we learned that atoms have a tiny, dense nucleus at their center. But the nucleus itself has its own structure – it's made of even smaller particles!

The same type of alpha-particle scattering experiments that revealed the nucleus also helped scientists figure out what's inside it. Let's meet the particles that live in the nucleus:

Particle Symbol Charge Mass Location
Proton p⁺ +1 ~1800× electron mass Nucleus
Neutron n⁰ 0 Slightly heavier than proton Nucleus
Electron e⁻ -1 1 (by comparison) Outside nucleus
🔬 Mind-Blowing Fact: The nucleus takes up almost no space, but contains 99.9% of the atom's mass!

If the nucleus were a marble, the atom would be a massive stadium – mostly empty space!

🔍 Discovering the Nuclear Particles

Protons – Rutherford's Discovery (1919)

In 1919, Ernest Rutherford discovered that when alpha particles strike certain light elements like nitrogen, they knock out hydrogen nuclei. He realized these were fundamental particles – and named them protons (from the Greek word for "first").

🔵 Proton: A nuclear particle with a positive charge equal to that of the electron and a mass more than 1800 times that of the electron. The number of protons in a nucleus gives the atom its atomic number (Z) and determines which element it is!

Neutrons – Chadwick's Discovery (1932)

👨‍🔬
James Chadwick (1891–1974)

British physicist who discovered the neutron using alpha-particle scattering experiments on beryllium.

When scientists shot alpha rays at beryllium metal, they got back a super-penetrating radiation. In 1932, Chadwick proved this radiation was made of neutral particles – neutrons!

⚪ Neutron: A nuclear particle with a mass almost identical to the proton but no electric charge. Neutrons act like "nuclear glue" – they help hold the positively charged protons together (otherwise they'd fly apart!).

🔢 Atomic Number and Mass Number

Every nucleus is characterized by two key numbers:

🔵 Atomic Number (Z): The number of protons in a nucleus. This is what makes an element unique – every atom of the same element has the same atomic number.

⚪ Mass Number (A): The total number of protons + neutrons in a nucleus.

A nuclide is an atom characterized by a specific atomic number and mass number. We write it like this:

A Z
X

Where X = element symbol, Z = atomic number, A = mass number

Example: Sodium

Naturally occurring sodium has:

  • 11 protons (atomic number Z = 11)
  • 12 neutrons

🧠 Remember: The number of protons (Z) tells you the element. Change Z and you change the element! Change neutrons and you get isotopes (same element, different mass).

🧬 Isotopes – Same Element, Different Mass

Isotopes are atoms whose nuclei have the same atomic number (Z) but different mass numbers (A). That means they have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

✍️ Writing Nuclide Symbols – Let's Practice!

Example 2.1: Finding the Nuclide Symbol

Problem: What is the nuclide symbol for a nucleus that contains 38 protons and 50 neutrons?

🧠 Problem Strategy

  • The number of protons = atomic number (Z)
  • Use Z to find the element symbol from the periodic table
  • Mass number (A) = protons + neutrons

✅ Solution

  1. Atomic number Z = 38 → that's Strontium (Sr)
  2. Mass number A = 38 + 50 = 88
  3. Nuclide symbol:
88 38
Sr

We can also call it strontium-88.

✏️ Exercise 2.1 – Try It Yourself!

A nucleus consists of 17 protons and 18 neutrons. What is its nuclide symbol?

Click for answer

Step 1: Z = 17 → that's Chlorine (Cl)

Step 2: A = 17 + 18 = 35

Step 3: Nuclide symbol:

Cl

Also called chlorine-35.

📝 Pro Tip: When writing elemental symbols, always capitalize only the first letter! (Co is cobalt, but CO is carbon monoxide – a whole different thing!)
