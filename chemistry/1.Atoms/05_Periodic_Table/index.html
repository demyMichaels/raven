Periodic Table of the Elements

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Identify periods and groups on the periodic table.
  • Find the main-group and transition elements on the periodic table.
  • Locate the alkali metal and halogen groups on the periodic table.
  • Recognize the portions of the periodic table that contain the metals, nonmetals, and metalloids (semimetals).

📜 The Genius Who Organized the Elements

👨‍🔬
Dmitri Mendeleev (1834–1907)

Russian chemist who, along with Germany's Lothar Meyer, independently discovered that when elements are arranged by atomic mass, their properties repeat in a regular pattern. Mendeleev even predicted the existence of undiscovered elements and left gaps for them in his table – and he was right!

In 1869, Mendeleev and Meyer made a huge breakthrough. They found that when they arranged elements in order of atomic mass, they could place them in rows so that elements in each column had similar properties. This was the first real periodic table!

Later, scientists realized that the true organizing principle is atomic number (number of protons), not atomic mass. When arranged by atomic number, all the inconsistencies disappeared. Genius!

📏 Periods – Horizontal Rows

A period is a horizontal row in the periodic table. Elements in the same period have the same number of electron shells.

  • Period 1: Just 2 elements – Hydrogen (H) and Helium (He)
  • Period 2: 8 elements – Lithium (Li) to Neon (Ne)
  • Period 3: 8 elements – Sodium (Na) to Argon (Ar)
  • Period 4: 18 elements – Potassium (K) to Krypton (Kr)
  • Period 5: 18 elements – Rubidium (Rb) to Xenon (Xe)
  • Period 6: 32 elements – includes the lanthanides (at the bottom)
  • Period 7: Still being completed – includes the actinides (at the bottom)

The two rows at the bottom (lanthanides and actinides) actually belong in periods 6 and 7 – they're placed there just to make the table fit on a page!

📋 Groups – Vertical Columns

A group is a vertical column. Elements in the same group have similar chemical properties because they have the same number of valence electrons.

There are two common numbering systems:

  • Traditional North American: Uses Roman numerals + A/B (e.g., Group IA, VIIA)
  • IUPAC (modern): Numbers 1-18 (simpler!)

Main-Group Elements (A Groups)

The A groups (Groups 1, 2, and 13-18) are called main-group elements or representative elements. They show a wide range of properties.

Group 1A Group 2A 3A-8A

Alkali Metals – Group 1A (Except H)

Li Na K Rb Cs Fr

These are soft, silvery metals that react violently with water! Sodium and potassium are classic examples. (Hydrogen is in Group 1 but is a gas – it's the odd one out!)

Halogens – Group 7A

F Cl Br I At

Highly reactive nonmetals that form salts with metals. Chlorine (gas) and bromine (red-brown liquid) are examples. Remember how sodium and chlorine reacted violently to make table salt? That's the halogen family!

Transition Elements (B Groups)

The B groups (Groups 3-12) are the transition elements – mostly shiny, colorful metals like iron (Fe), copper (Cu), and gold (Au).

Sc Ti V Cr Mn Fe Co Ni Cu Zn

Inner Transition Elements

The two rows at the bottom are the inner transition elements:

  • Lanthanides (first row) – atomic numbers 58-71
  • Actinides (second row) – atomic numbers 90-103

🔩 Metals, Nonmetals, and Metalloids

Look at the periodic table – see that heavy "staircase" line? It divides the elements into three big categories:

Metals (Left of the line)

Shiny, good conductors of heat and electricity, malleable (can be hammered into sheets), ductile (can be drawn into wire). Most elements are metals! Except for mercury, all are solids at room temperature.

Nonmetals (Right of the line)

Dull, poor conductors, brittle (if solid). Many are gases (oxygen, chlorine), some are solids (carbon, sulfur), and one is a liquid (bromine).

Metalloids (Bordering the line)

Elements with properties of both metals and nonmetals. They're semiconductors – they conduct electricity better at higher temperatures. Silicon (Si) and germanium (Ge) are used in computer chips!

🤔 Concept Check 2.3: Consider He, Ne, and Ar. Why are they in the same group?

Think about it...

They're all noble gases (Group 8A)! They have full outer electron shells, making them extremely unreactive. They share similar properties – colorless, odorless gases that rarely form compounds.

📊 Quick Summary

Term Definition
Period Horizontal row – elements have same number of electron shells
Group Vertical column – elements have similar properties (same valence electrons)
Main-Group Elements A groups (1,2,13-18) – representative elements
Transition Elements B groups (3-12) – colorful metals
Alkali Metals Group 1A (except H) – super reactive with water
Halogens Group 7A – reactive nonmetals that form salts
Metals Left of staircase – shiny, conductive, malleable
Nonmetals Right of staircase – dull, brittle, poor conductors
Metalloids Bordering staircase – semiconductors (like Si, Ge)
