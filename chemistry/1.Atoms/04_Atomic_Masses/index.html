Topic 1.4

Atomic Masses

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define atomic mass unit and atomic mass.
  • Describe how a mass spectrometer can be used to determine the fractional abundance of the isotopes of an element.
  • Determine the atomic mass of an element from the isotopic masses and fractional abundances.

⚖️ What Exactly is Atomic Mass?

One of Dalton's key ideas was that each element has its own characteristic atomic mass. But here's the twist – as we learned in Topic 1.3, most elements are actually mixtures of isotopes! So what mass do we use?

📌 Atomic Mass (Atomic Weight): The average mass of an element's atoms, taking into account all its naturally occurring isotopes and their abundances. It's what you see on the periodic table!

Think of it like calculating your final grade in a class – different assignments have different "weights." The atomic mass is the weighted average of all the isotopes of an element.

🧠 Historical Note: Dalton couldn't weigh individual atoms (obviously!). Instead, he found the relative masses of atoms compared to each other. For example, he burned hydrogen in oxygen and found that 1.0000 g of hydrogen reacts with 7.9367 g of oxygen. Since water is H₂O (2 H atoms for every 1 O atom), the oxygen atom must be 15.87 times heavier than hydrogen!

📏 Atomic Mass Units (amu)

Today, we use a carbon-12 standard. One carbon-12 atom is assigned a mass of exactly 12 atomic mass units.

📌 Atomic Mass Unit (amu): Exactly 1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom. This is the standard unit for measuring atomic masses.

So when you see on the periodic table that hydrogen has an atomic mass of 1.008 amu, that means a hydrogen atom is about 1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom.

🔬 The Mass Spectrometer – Weighing Atoms

How do we actually measure atomic masses? With an amazing instrument called a mass spectrometer!

Figure 2.10: Mass spectrometer diagram showing sample injection, ionization, acceleration, and magnetic deflection

How It Works:

  1. Sample injector port introduces the sample
  2. Electron gun ionizes the atoms (gives them a positive charge)
  3. Accelerated ion beam speeds them up
  4. Magnetic field bends the ions – lighter ions bend MORE, heavier ions bend LESS
  5. Detectors measure how many ions hit each spot – creating a mass spectrum

Neon Mass Spectrum – Can You Spot All Three Isotopes?

When we run neon through a mass spectrometer, we see THREE peaks – one for each isotope. But neon-21 is super rare (only 0.27%!), so we need a logarithmic scale to see it:

📊 Neon Mass Spectrum log scale
✨ See it? Neon-21 is the tiny green bar – only 0.27% of natural neon! The log scale lets us see all three isotopes at once.

The mass spectrum gives us two crucial pieces of information:

  • Mass of each isotope (where the peak appears – x-axis)
  • Fractional abundance (how tall the peak is – y-axis, relative number of atoms)

📌 Fractional Abundance: The fraction of the total number of atoms that is a particular isotope. For neon:

  • Neon-20: 0.9051 (90.51%)
  • Neon-21: 0.0027 (0.27%)
  • Neon-22: 0.0922 (9.22%)

🧮 Calculating Atomic Mass – The Weighted Average

To calculate the atomic mass, we do a weighted average:

Atomic Mass = Σ (isotopic mass × fractional abundance)

Think of it like your final grade – if exams are worth 70% and labs are worth 30%, you'd calculate:

(exam grade × 0.70) + (lab grade × 0.30) = final grade

Exactly the same idea with isotopes!

Example 2.2: Chromium

Chromium has four naturally occurring isotopes:

Mass Number Isotopic Mass (amu) Fractional Abundance
50 49.9461 0.0435
52 51.9405 0.8379
53 52.9407 0.0950
54 53.9389 0.0236

🧠 Problem Strategy

Multiply each isotopic mass by its fractional abundance, then add them all up!

49.9461 × 0.0435 = 2.17 amu
51.9405 × 0.8379 = 43.52 amu
52.9407 × 0.0950 = 5.03 amu
53.9389 × 0.0236 = 1.27 amu
Total: 51.99 amu
Atomic Mass of Chromium = 51.99 amu

✅ Quick Check: The most abundant isotope is Chromium-52 (83.79% abundance) with mass 51.9405 amu. Our answer (51.99 amu) is very close – that's a good sign!

✏️ Exercise 2.2 – Try It Yourself!

Chlorine consists of the following isotopes:

Isotope Mass (amu) Fractional Abundance
Chlorine-35 34.96885 0.75771
Chlorine-37 36.96590 0.24229

What is the atomic mass of chlorine?

Click for answer
34.96885 × 0.75771 = 26.50 amu
36.96590 × 0.24229 = 8.96 amu
Total: 35.46 amu

Atomic mass of chlorine = 35.46 amu

(Check the periodic table – it says 35.45 amu. Close enough!)

📊 Quick Summary

Term Definition
Atomic Mass Unit (amu) 1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom
Atomic Mass (Atomic Weight) Weighted average mass of all naturally occurring isotopes
Fractional Abundance The fraction of atoms that are a particular isotope
Mass Spectrometer Instrument that measures isotopic masses and abundances

📝 Formula to Remember: Atomic Mass = (mass₁ × abundance₁) + (mass₂ × abundance₂) + ...
