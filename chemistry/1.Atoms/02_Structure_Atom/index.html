The Structure of the Atom

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Describe Thomson's experiment in which he discovered the electron.
  • Describe Rutherford's experiment that led to the nuclear model of the atom.

⚛️ What's Inside an Atom?

You might remember from Topic 1.1 that Dalton thought atoms were tiny, solid, indivisible spheres. But around 1900, scientists started to realize that atoms actually have smaller parts inside them!

Experiments showed that an atom is made of two main things:

  • The Nucleus – the tiny, super-dense center of the atom. It's positively charged and contains almost all of the atom's mass (like 99.9% of it!).
  • Electrons – super-light, negatively charged particles that zoom around the nucleus in the empty space around it.

But how did scientists figure this out? Let's meet the two pioneers who cracked the code!

🔬 Discovery of the Electron – J.J. Thomson (1897)

👨‍🔬
J.J. Thomson

British physicist who proved that atoms weren't indivisible after all. He discovered the first subatomic particle – the electron!

The Cathode Ray Tube Experiment

Thomson used a cool piece of equipment called a cathode ray tube. It's kind of like an old-school TV tube. Here's how it worked:

Watch the Experiment:

This demo shows how an electron beam bends in a magnetic field – just like Thomson observed!

Formation of cathode rays: Cathode rays leave the cathode (negative electrode) and are accelerated toward the anode (positive electrode). Some rays pass through the hole in the anode to form a beam, which is then bent by the electric plates in the tube.

What Thomson Observed:

  • When he turned on the high voltage, a greenish glow appeared in the tube.
  • This glow was caused by cathode rays shooting from the negative electrode (cathode) to the positive electrode (anode).
  • The beam bent toward a positively charged plate and away from a negatively charged plate.
  • Magnets also made the beam bend – just like they would bend a stream of charged particles!

What Thomson Concluded:

  • Cathode rays must be made of negatively charged particles – because they were attracted to positive plates.
  • He called these particles "corpuscles" (we now call them electrons!).
  • The rays were the same no matter what metal he used for the electrodes. That meant electrons are a part of EVERY atom – they're universal building blocks!
🧠 Fun Fact: Thomson could figure out the ratio of the electron's mass to its charge (m/e), but he couldn't get the mass or charge alone. That puzzle was solved later by Robert Millikan (more on him in the Improve Your Grade section!).

🎯 The Nuclear Model – Ernest Rutherford (1911)

👨‍🔬
Ernest Rutherford

New Zealand-born British physicist who discovered that atoms have a tiny, dense nucleus. He's often called the "father of nuclear physics."

The Gold Foil Experiment (Geiger and Marsden)

Rutherford's team (two of his students, Hans Geiger and Ernest Marsden) shot tiny positively charged particles called alpha particles at an ultra-thin sheet of gold foil.

Discovery of the Nucleus

Quick & Simple Explanation

What They Expected:

Based on Thomson's "plum pudding" model (where electrons were scattered throughout a positive blob), they thought the alpha particles would just zip straight through with maybe a tiny bend.

What Actually Happened (Mind-blowing!):

  • Most alpha particles went straight through – like nothing was there.
  • A few (about 1 in 8,000) bounced off at crazy angles – some even bounced straight back toward the source!

Rutherford later said: "It was almost as incredible as if you fired a 15-inch shell at a piece of tissue paper and it came back and hit you." 🤯

What Rutherford Concluded:

  • Most of the atom must be empty space – that's why most alpha particles went straight through.
  • The atom has a tiny, dense, positively charged center – the nucleus. This is what bounced the alpha particles back (positive repels positive!).
  • Electrons must orbit around this nucleus in the empty space.

Mind-Blowing Size Comparison:

  • Nucleus diameter: about 10⁻¹⁵ m (super, super tiny!)
  • Atom diameter: about 10⁻¹⁰ m – that's 100,000 times bigger!
  • If the nucleus were a golf ball, the atom would be about 3 miles across! ⛳
💡 Why It Matters: Rutherford's model completely changed how we see the atom. Instead of a solid blob, we now picture it as mostly empty space with a tiny, dense nucleus – like a fly in a stadium!

📝 Quick Summary

Scientist Experiment Discovery
J.J. Thomson Cathode Ray Tube Discovered the electron – a negatively charged particle inside atoms.
Ernest Rutherford Gold Foil Experiment Discovered the nucleus – a tiny, dense, positively charged center of the atom.
