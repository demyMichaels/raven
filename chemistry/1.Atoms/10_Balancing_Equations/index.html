Topic 1.10

Balancing Chemical Equations

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Determine if a chemical reaction is balanced.
  • Master the techniques for balancing chemical equations. Example 2.12

⚖️ What Does It Mean to Balance an Equation?

When the coefficients in a chemical equation are correctly given, the numbers of atoms of each element are equal on both sides of the arrow. The equation is then said to be balanced. That a chemical equation should be balanced follows from atomic theory. A chemical reaction involves simply a recombination of the atoms; none are destroyed and none are created.

Consider the burning of natural gas, which is composed mostly of methane, CH₄. Using atomic theory, you describe this as the chemical reaction of one molecule of methane, CH₄, with two molecules of oxygen, O₂, to form one molecule of carbon dioxide, CO₂, and two molecules of water, H₂O.

CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
one molecule of methane + two molecules of oxygen → one molecule of carbon dioxide + two molecules of water

🔥 Balancing by Inspection – The Trial-and-Error Method

Before you can write a balanced chemical equation for a reaction, you must determine by experiment those substances that are reactants and those that are products. You must also determine the formulas of each substance. Once you know these things, you can write the balanced chemical equation.

As an example, consider the burning of propane gas (Figure 2.29). By experiment, you determine that propane reacts with oxygen in air to give carbon dioxide and water. You also determine from experiment that the formulas of propane, oxygen, carbon dioxide, and water are C₃H₈, O₂, CO₂, and H₂O, respectively. Then you can write:

C₃H₈ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O
Unbalanced equation

This equation is not balanced because the coefficients that give the relative number of molecules involved have not yet been determined. To balance the equation, you select coefficients that will make the numbers of atoms of each element equal on both sides of the equation.

Step-by-Step Balancing of Propane Combustion

Step 1: Because there are three carbon atoms on the left side of the equation (C₃H₈), you must have three carbon atoms on the right. This is achieved by writing 3 for the coefficient of CO₂.

1C₃H₈ + O₂ → 3CO₂ + H₂O

(We have written the coefficient for C₃H₈ to show that it is now determined; normally when the coefficient is 1, it is omitted.)

Step 2: Similarly, there are eight hydrogen atoms on the left (in C₃H₈), so you write 4 for the coefficient of H₂O.

1C₃H₈ + O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O

Step 3: The coefficients on the right are now determined, and there are ten oxygen atoms on this side of the equation: 6 from the three CO₂ molecules and 4 from the four H₂O molecules. You now write 5 for the coefficient of O₂.

C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O

(We drop the coefficient 1 from C₃H₈ and have the balanced equation.)

C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O
Balanced equation for propane combustion

It is a good idea to check your work by counting the number of atoms of each element on the left side and then on the right side of the equation.

The combustion of propane can be equally well represented by:

2C₃H₈ + 10O₂ → 6CO₂ + 8H₂O

in which the coefficients of the previous equation have been doubled. Usually, however, it is preferable to write the coefficients so that they are the smallest whole numbers possible. When we refer to a balanced equation, we will assume that this is the case unless otherwise specified.

💡 The Golden Rule of Balancing

⚖️ Balance first the atoms for elements that occur in only one substance on each side of the equation.

The method we have outlined—called balancing by inspection—is essentially a trial-and-error method. It can be made easier, however, by observing the rule above.

The usefulness of this rule is illustrated in the next example. Remember that in any formula, such as Fe₂(SO₄)₃, a subscript to the right of the parentheses multiplies each subscript within the parentheses. Thus, Fe₂(SO₄)₃ represents 4 × 3 oxygen atoms. Remember also that you cannot change a subscript in any formula; only the coefficients can be altered to balance an equation.

📝 Example 2.12: Balancing Simple Equations

Balance the following equations:

  1. a. H₃PO₃ → H₃PO₄ + PH₃
  2. b. Ca + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + H₂
  3. c. Fe₂(SO₄)₃ + NH₃ + H₂O → Fe(OH)₃ + (NH₄)₂SO₄

🧠 Problem Strategy

Look at the chemical equation for atoms of elements that occur in only one substance on each side of the equation. Begin by balancing the equation in one of these atoms. Use the number of atoms on the left side of the arrow as the coefficient of the substance containing that element on the right side, and vice versa. After balancing one element in an equation, the rest become easier.

✅ Solution a. H₃PO₃ → H₃PO₄ + PH₃

Oxygen occurs in just one of the products (H₃PO₄). It is therefore easiest to balance O atoms first. To do this, note that H₃PO₃ has three O atoms; use 3 as the coefficient of H₃PO₄ on the right side. There are four O atoms in H₃PO₄ on the right side; use 4 as the coefficient of H₃PO₃ on the left side.

4H₃PO₃ → 3H₃PO₄ + PH₃

This equation is now also balanced in P and H atoms. Thus, the balanced equation is:

4H₃PO₃ → 3H₃PO₄ + PH₃

✅ Solution b. Ca + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + H₂

The equation is balanced in Ca atoms as it stands.

1Ca + H₂O → 1Ca(OH)₂ + H₂

O atoms occur in only one reactant and in only one product, so they are balanced next.

1Ca + 2H₂O → 1Ca(OH)₂ + H₂

The equation is now also balanced in H atoms. Thus, the answer is:

Ca + 2H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + H₂

✅ Solution c. Fe₂(SO₄)₃ + NH₃ + H₂O → Fe(OH)₃ + (NH₄)₂SO₄

Step 1: To balance the equation in Fe atoms, you write:

1Fe₂(SO₄)₃ + NH₃ + H₂O → 2Fe(OH)₃ + (NH₄)₂SO₄

Step 2: You balance the S atoms by placing the coefficient 3 for (NH₄)₂SO₄.

1Fe₂(SO₄)₃ + 6NH₃ + H₂O → 2Fe(OH)₃ + 3(NH₄)₂SO₄

Step 3: Now you balance the N atoms.

1Fe₂(SO₄)₃ + 6NH₃ + H₂O → 2Fe(OH)₃ + 3(NH₄)₂SO₄

Step 4: To balance the O atoms, you first count the number of O atoms on the right (18). Then you count the number of O atoms in substances on the left with known coefficients. There are 12 O's in Fe₂(SO₄)₃; hence, the number of remaining O's (in H₂O) must be 18 − 12 = 6.

1Fe₂(SO₄)₃ + 6NH₃ + 6H₂O → 2Fe(OH)₃ + 3(NH₄)₂SO₄

Step 5: Finally, note that the equation is now balanced in H atoms. The answer is:

Fe₂(SO₄)₃ + 6NH₃ + 6H₂O → 2Fe(OH)₃ + 3(NH₄)₂SO₄

✅ Answer Check

As a final step when balancing chemical equations, always count the number of atoms of each element on both sides of the equation to make certain that they are equal.

📊 Quick Summary

Rule Explanation
Balance one element at a time Start with elements that appear in only one reactant and one product
Use coefficients, not subscripts Never change the subscripts in a formula – only add coefficients in front
Check your work Count atoms on both sides to verify they're equal
Smallest whole numbers Reduce coefficients to the smallest whole numbers possible

📝 Key Takeaway: Balancing equations is like a puzzle – you're making sure the same number of each type of atom appears on both sides, because atoms are neither created nor destroyed in chemical reactions!
