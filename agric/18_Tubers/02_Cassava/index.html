Cassava

Manihot esculenta Crantz
Family: Euphorbiaceae
Common Names
Manioc, Yuca, Tapioca
Local Names (Nigeria)
Yoruba: Ege, Pakala; Hausa: Rogo; Igbo: Akpu, Ji akpu
Major Varieties
TMS 30572, TME 419, 98/0505, 4(2)1425 (sweet), 60506 (bitter)

Learning Objectives

  • apply the different methods of crop propagation, husbandry, harvesting, processing and storage for cassava
  • identify common diseases and pests and their effects on crop yield
  • determine the economic importance of cassava
  • relate cassava's importance to national economic development

📝 1. Identification & Classification

Taxonomic Classification

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Magnoliophyta
  • Class: Magnoliopsida
  • Order: Malpighiales
  • Family: Euphorbiaceae
  • Genus: Manihot
  • Species: M. esculenta

Botanical Description

  • Growth habit: Perennial shrub, grown as annual
  • Height: 1-5 meters tall
  • Life cycle: 6-24 months depending on variety

Cassava Types

  • Sweet cassava: Low cyanide (<50 mg/kg), can be eaten after simple boiling
  • Bitter cassava: High cyanide (>100 mg/kg), requires extensive processing

🌿 2. Gross Morphology

Root System

  • Fibrous roots: Thin, hair-like, for water and nutrient absorption
  • Tuberous roots (storage roots): 5-10 per plant, swollen with starch
  • Root color: Brown outer skin (periderm), white/cream/pinkish flesh
  • Root length: 30-60 cm, can reach 100 cm in deep soils

Stem

  • Type: Erect, woody, cylindrical
  • Color: Varies by variety - brown, gray, silver, greenish
  • Nodes: Prominent leaf scars at nodes
  • Branches: Branched at top
  • Used for: Planting material (stem cuttings)

Leaves

  • Arrangement: Alternate, spirally arranged
  • Type: Palmate compound with 3-7 lobes (usually 5-7)
  • Petiole: Long (up to 30 cm), slender, reddish or green
  • Leaf blade: Lobes lanceolate, entire margins
  • Color: Dark green above, lighter beneath
  • Dropping: Leaves drop during dry season (dormancy)

Flowers and Fruits

  • Inflorescence: Panicles, monoecious (male and female flowers on same plant)
  • Male flowers: Smaller, numerous, at branch tips
  • Female flowers: Larger, fewer, at base of inflorescence
  • Fruit: Capsule, 6-angled, 1-1.5 cm diameter
  • Seeds: 3 per capsule, ovoid, mottled brown
  • Note: Seed propagation rarely used commercially

🧬 3. Anatomy of Storage Organs

Structure of Cassava Root (Cross-section)

  • Periderm (Skin): Outer protective layer, corky, brown, 1-2 mm thick
  • Cortex (Peel): Middle layer, 2-3 mm thick, contains:
    • Cortical parenchyma
    • Laticifers (latex vessels)
    • Cyanogenic glucosides (linamarin and lotaustralin)
  • Vascular tissues:
    • Phloem: transports sugars
    • Xylem: transports water and minerals, forms star-shaped pattern
  • Parenchyma (Pulp/Flesh): Inner storage tissue, makes up bulk of root, contains:
    • Starch granules in amyloplasts
    • 25-35% starch content by fresh weight

Cyanogenic Glucosides

  • Linamarin: Main cyanogenic compound (93%)
  • Lotaustralin: Minor compound (7%)
  • Enzyme linamarase: Breaks down glucosides to release hydrogen cyanide (HCN)
  • Distribution: Highest in peel and cortex
  • Processing necessity: Removes cyanide through grating, fermenting, drying, cooking

🌱 4. Methods of Propagation

Asexual (Vegetative) Propagation - Primary Method

  • Material: Stem cuttings (stakes, setts)
  • Cutting size: 20-30 cm long, 2-3 cm diameter
  • Nodes required: 5-7 nodes per cutting
  • Source: Mature stems from 8-18 month old plants
  • Cutting preparation:
    • Use sharp knife, slanting cut
    • Avoid bruising
    • Remove leaves
    • Plant within 2-3 days (can be stored in shade for up to 2 weeks)

Planting Methods

  • Vertical: Inserted upright with 2-3 nodes above ground - for dry areas
  • Slanting: At 45° angle - most common
  • Horizontal: Laid flat, covered with 5 cm soil - for wet areas

Seed Rate and Spacing

  • Seed rate: 10,000-15,000 cuttings per hectare
  • Spacing:
    • 1m x 1m = 10,000 plants/ha
    • 1m x 0.8m = 12,500 plants/ha
    • 0.8m x 0.8m = 15,625 plants/ha (high density)

Sexual Propagation (Breeding only)

  • Using true seeds from capsules
  • Used in breeding programs for new varieties
  • Not used for commercial planting due to genetic variation

👨‍🌾 5. Husbandry Practices

Land Preparation

  • Clearing: Remove vegetation, stumps, rocks
  • Primary tillage: Plough (20-30 cm depth)
  • Secondary tillage: Harrow to break clods
  • Ridging: Make ridges 30-50 cm high, spaced 1m apart
  • Mounding: In some areas, especially for sole cropping

Planting Time

  • Early season: March-May (with onset of rains)
  • Late season: September-October
  • Dry season: With irrigation where available

Fertilizer Application

  • NPK 15:15:15: 400-600 kg/ha
  • Apply: 4-6 weeks after planting
  • Organic manure: 5-10 tons/ha if available
  • Potassium: Especially important for root development

Weeding

  • Frequency: 2-3 weedings in first 3 months
  • First weeding: 3-4 weeks after planting
  • Second weeding: 8-10 weeks after planting
  • Third weeding: As needed before canopy closure
  • Methods: Manual (hoe), mechanical, or chemical (herbicides)

Gap Filling

  • Replace failed stands 2-3 weeks after planting
  • Use same variety, same age cuttings

Mulching

  • Conserves soil moisture
  • Suppresses weed growth
  • Adds organic matter when decomposed

Intercropping

  • Common with maize, melon, vegetables in early stages
  • Beneficial for risk management and land use efficiency

🌾 6. Harvesting

Maturity Indicators

  • Early maturing varieties: 6-12 months
  • Late maturing varieties: 18-24 months
  • Yellowing and dropping of leaves
  • Bark cracking on roots
  • Root size: 30-60 cm long, 5-15 cm diameter

Harvesting Method

  • Loosen soil around base with hoe or cutlass
  • Pull stems to lift roots
  • Cut roots from stem base
  • Remove soil, sort, and grade
  • Care: Avoid bruising roots (accelerates deterioration)

🏭 7. Processing Methods

Product Processing Steps Uses
Garri Peel → Wash → Grate → Ferment (2-3 days) → Press → Sieve → Fry → Cool → Package Eaten soaked in water, with soup, or as dry garri
Fufu (Akpu) Peel → Wash → Cut → Soak (3-5 days) → Sieve → Ferment → Press → Cook → Pound Stiff dough eaten with soup
Lafun Peel → Cut → Dry (sun) → Mill into flour → Sieve Flour for amala (swallow)
Cassava Flour Peel → Chip → Dry → Mill → Sieve Baking, confectionery, composite flour
Tapioca Peel → Grate → Roast → Granulate → Dry Breakfast cereal, thickener
Starch Peel → Grate → Extract with water → Settle → Decant → Dry → Mill Industrial uses, food processing
High Quality Cassava Flour (HQCF) Peel → Chip → Dry → Mill → Fine sieve 10% substitution in wheat flour (bread)

📦 8. Storage Methods

Fresh Roots Storage

  • Duration: 2-7 days only (rapid post-harvest deterioration)
  • Cause of deterioration: Physiological (vascular streaking) and microbial rot
  • In-ground storage: Leave roots in soil and harvest as needed (up to 2 years)
  • Wax coating: Can extend storage to 2-3 weeks
  • Cold storage: 0-4°C, 85-90% RH for 1-2 weeks

Processed Products Storage

  • Garri: Store in airtight containers, dry place, up to 12 months
  • Flour: Store in cool, dry place; up to 6 months
  • Fufu: Can be dried and stored as flour
  • Starch: Store in sealed bags, dry conditions

Stem Cuttings Storage

  • Store in shade, keep moist
  • Can be stored vertically in bundles
  • Duration: 2-4 weeks maximum

🦠 9. Common Diseases

Disease Causal Agent Symptoms Effects on Yield
Cassava Mosaic Disease (CMD) Virus (begomovirus, genus: Cassava mosaic virus) Leaf chlorosis, distortion, stunting, reduced leaf size 20-80% yield loss depending on variety
Cassava Brown Streak Disease (CBSD) Virus (ipomovirus, genus: Cassava brown streak virus) Yellowing of leaves, brown necrotic streaks on stem, brown rot in roots Up to 100% loss of marketable roots
Anthracnose Fungus (Colletotrichum gloeosporioides) Stem cankers, dieback, defoliation, stem breakage 10-30% yield loss
Bacterial Blight Bacteria (Xanthomonas axonopodis pv. manihotis) Angular leaf spots, wilting, stem exudate, dieback 20-50% yield loss
Root Rot Fungi (Phytophthora, Fusarium, Rhizoctonia spp.) Root decay, wilting, plant death Total loss in wet conditions
Leaf Spot (Cercospora) Fungi (Cercospora spp.) Brown spots on leaves, defoliation 10-20% yield loss

🐛 10. Common Pests

Pest Type Symptoms/Damage Effects on Yield
Cassava Green Mite
(Mononychellus tanajoa)		 Mite Leaf distortion, chlorosis, defoliation, stunting 20-50% yield loss
Cassava Mealybug
(Phenacoccus manihoti)		 Insect (Hemiptera) Shoot tip damage, leaf curling, sooty mold, stunting 30-80% yield loss in severe infestations

(Aonidomytilus albus)		 Insect (Hemiptera) Suck sap from stems, weaken plant, reduce root yield 10-30% yield loss
Whiteflies
(Bemisia tabaci)		 Insect (Hemiptera) Direct feeding damage, virus vectors (CMD) Up to 50% loss + virus transmission
Grasshoppers/Locusts Insect (Orthoptera) Defoliation during outbreaks Variable, up to 100% in outbreaks
Rodents (Rats) Vertebrate Damage to stored roots and stem cuttings Post-harvest losses

(Meloidogyne spp.)		 Roundworms Root galls, reduced root size, secondary rot 10-30% yield loss

Control Measures

  • Cultural: Use clean planting material, crop rotation, rogue infected plants, intercropping
  • Biological: Natural enemies (ladybirds for mealybugs, predatory mites for green mite)
  • Chemical: Pesticides (acaricides for mites, insecticides for mealybugs)
  • Resistant varieties: TMS varieties resistant to CMD, some tolerant to CBSD
  • Phytosanitary measures: Quarantine to prevent CBSD spread

💰 11. Economic Importance of Cassava

Food Security

  • Staple food for over 800 million people worldwide
  • Major source of dietary carbohydrates in tropical Africa
  • Provides 30-40% of caloric intake in many West African countries
  • Can be stored in ground for up to 2 years (famine reserve)
  • Food products: Garri, fufu, lafun, tapioca, flour, abacha

Industrial Uses

  • Starch production: Textile, paper, adhesive, pharmaceutical, food industries
  • Biofuel: Ethanol production from cassava (10-15% blending in petrol)
  • Animal feed: Peel, leaves, and pulp as livestock feed
  • Sweeteners: Glucose syrup, dextrins, maltodextrin
  • Biodegradable plastics: Cassava starch-based bioplastics

Employment and Income

  • Provides livelihood for over 45 million Nigerians
  • Low capital requirement - accessible to smallholder farmers
  • Value chain creates jobs in processing, transportation, marketing
  • Women comprise 70-80% of cassava processors
  • Over 300,000 small-scale processing enterprises in Nigeria

Export Earnings

  • Nigeria is world's largest cassava producer (over 60 million tons/year)
  • Export products: Garri, flour, starch, chips, pellets
  • High-quality cassava flour for bakeries (10% substitution in wheat flour)
  • Cassava chips exported to China for ethanol production
  • Potential to earn billions in foreign exchange with value addition

🇳🇬 12. Cassava in National Economic Development

Contribution to GDP

  • Accounts for over 20% of agricultural GDP in Nigeria
  • Contributes approximately 4-5% to total GDP
  • Over 45 million Nigerians engaged in cassava value chain
  • Annual production: 60+ million metric tons (Nigeria #1 globally)

Import Substitution

  • High-quality cassava flour (HQCF) replaces wheat flour - saves foreign exchange
  • Cassava starch replaces imported starch
  • Government policy: 10% cassava flour inclusion in bread (Presidential Initiative)
  • Reduces dependence on imported grains and wheat

Government Initiatives

  • Presidential Cassava Initiative: Boost production and processing
  • Cassava Bread Fund: Promote cassava-wheat composite bread
  • Export incentives: Promote cassava exports
  • Research support: IITA, NRCRI (Umudike) developing improved varieties
  • Agricultural Transformation Agenda: Value chain approach

Rural Development

  • Cassava processing creates rural enterprises
  • Reduces rural-urban migration
  • Empowers women (majority of processors)
  • Improves rural infrastructure through ADP programs
  • Creates market linkages and value addition at village level
